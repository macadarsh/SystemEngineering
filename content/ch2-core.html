Why organise work as a life cycle?

Systems engineering exists to realise a system successfully while balancing competing stakeholder objectives — cost, schedule, performance, risk, safety, security, and many others that pull in different directions. No team can hold the whole of a complex system in mind at once, so the work is broken into a sequence of transformational stages. Each stage advances the system from a fuzzy idea toward a concrete, operating reality, and at the boundary between stages the team pauses to ask three blunt questions: are the system's characteristics on track, is the remaining risk acceptable, and is the system ready to move on? By analogy with the phases a living organism passes through, the complete set of stages is the system life cycle.

Thinking in terms of a life cycle does two useful things. First, it forces the team to consider the whole of life — not just design and build, but how the system will be produced, operated, supported for years or decades, and finally retired and disposed of. Decisions made cheaply in the early stages — a choice of architecture, a materials decision, a maintenance concept — dominate the cost and feasibility of everything that follows, which is why the cheapest moment to fix a problem is almost always the earliest. Second, it gives managers and engineers a shared map: a common language for where a project is, what should already be true, and what comes next. That shared map is what lets large, distributed teams coordinate without re-negotiating first principles every week.

Life-cycle stages in depth

A widely used set of stages, drawn from ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 and 24748, spans conception through retirement. The names below are generic; commercial, defence, and space sectors each relabel them, and the time spent in each varies enormously — a consumer product may reach the field in a year or two and then remain in service for several years, while infrastructure or aerospace systems may spend a decade in development and half a century in service.

  • Concept — explore the problem or opportunity, assess feasibility, study candidate solution concepts, and refine stakeholder needs. The emphasis is on understanding what is needed and why before committing to a particular solution, so that the expensive work of development is aimed at the right target.
  • Development — turn the chosen concept into a defined system: elaborate requirements and architecture, complete the design, and build and verify the system and its elements. Most definitional engineering happens here.
  • Production — manufacture or construct the system and its elements, often repeatedly, with attention to quality, repeatability, yield, and unit cost.
  • Utilization — operate the system in its intended environment to deliver the services stakeholders wanted. For long-lived systems, this is by far the longest stage.
  • Support — provide the logistics, maintenance, training, and services that keep the system operating effectively. Utilization and Support typically run concurrently and account for most of a long-lived system's total cost.
  • Retirement — withdraw the system or its elements from service and dispose of them responsibly, handling waste, hazardous materials, decommissioning, and any critical disposal needs.

Crucially, stages are not a one-way conveyor belt. They can be entered more than once, they can overlap, and they frequently run in parallel. A system may be in Utilization while an upgraded version is still in Development; a product line may loop back through Concept for its next generation while the current generation is still being produced and supported. This is why life-cycle diagrams often show stages repeating or branching rather than as a single straight line, and why “which stage are we in?” sometimes has more than one correct answer at once.

Multiple, interacting life cycles

Real programmes rarely contain a single life cycle. The major system elements each have their own life cycles; the enabling systems — the test rigs, factories, training systems, and support tools that make the system possible but are not part of it — have theirs; and in a system-of-systems each constituent system runs an independent life cycle under separate management and funding. The systems engineer's job includes integrating these life cycles so that the right things exist at the right time — for example, ensuring the production facility, the spares pipeline, and the trained operators are all ready when the system enters Utilization. A brilliant system that arrives before its factory or its trained users is not yet a capability.

Decision gates

Progress between stages is controlled by decision gates — risk-managing checkpoints, usually placed at the start and end of a stage and often run as project milestones or reviews. A gate is fundamentally a moment of honest accounting. Its goals are to confirm that the system's maturity is on track, that the evolving deliverables still satisfy the business case, that resources remain sufficient, that open issues are being addressed, and — above all — that the risk of proceeding is acceptable. Gates exist so that commitment grows in deliberate, reviewable steps rather than all at once.

At each gate, decision-makers choose among a small, defined set of options: proceed to the next stage; continue in the current stage, perhaps after rework; return to an earlier stage to fix something fundamental; hold the project pending new information; or terminate it. Approval should rest on evidence against agreed criteria, not on optimism or on how much has already been spent. Entry criteria typically check that prerequisites are met and resources are available; exit criteria check that the stage's objectives have genuinely been achieved.

A recurring theme is that the formality and frequency of gates is itself a critical design choice. Too few or too superficial, and problems accumulate undetected until they are expensive to fix; too many or too heavy, and the project drowns in review bureaucracy and loses momentum. Agile and iterative approaches do not abandon gating — they still make go/no-go decisions — but they make them more frequently, at smaller scope, and with lighter ceremony, so that steering happens continuously instead of at a handful of dramatic milestones.

Technical reviews and audits

Gates are frequently informed by technical reviews and audits. A technical review assesses a project's progress against its technical requirements at logical transition points, using pre-agreed entry and success criteria; it asks whether the system is being built right so far. An audit is a more forensic check that the products and their definition information genuinely conform to requirements and to the released, baselined configuration. Both are planned in advance in the Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP), and both are tied to the maturing technical baselines — functional, allocated, and product. A representative sequence includes the System Requirements Review (SRR), System Functional Review (SFR), Preliminary Design Review (PDR), Critical Design Review (CDR), Test Readiness Review (TRR), System Verification Review (SVR), Production Readiness Review (PRR), and the Functional and Physical Configuration Audits (FCA and PCA). The exact set is always tailored to the project and the life-cycle model in use.

Life-cycle model approaches

A life-cycle model is the framework within which stages and their transitions are actually planned and executed. The handbook groups models into three families, separated by three questions: how much of the requirements are known at the start, how many development iterations are planned, and whether there will be multiple deployments. Those three levers — requirements certainty, iteration count, and deployment count — are what a programme really decides when it picks a way of working.

Sequential methods

Sequential models flow through linear stages, each building on the deliverables of the previous one (with feedback loops), and ending in a single delivery. Their virtues are predictability, stability, repeatability, and high assurance — qualities that suit large, coordinated teams and safety- or security-critical products where the requirements are well understood up front and where certification demands a documented, traceable trail. The classic illustration is the waterfall model (Royce, 1970), useful as much for exposing the danger of late requirements changes as for prescribing a method. The systems-engineering staple is the SE Vee model: the left leg decomposes and defines (stakeholder requirements → system requirements → architecture → element designs), the bottom is implementation, and the right leg integrates, verifies, and validates back up the hierarchy. A key insight is that each definition activity on the left is deliberately paired with the verification or validation activity on the right that will later confirm it, which is why test thinking begins early. Even inside the Vee, the underlying processes are applied concurrently, iteratively, and recursively at each level, so the Vee is a map of relationships, not a strict calendar.

The trade-off is rigidity: because the model assumes the requirements are essentially settled before design begins, late changes are disruptive and costly. That is precisely why sequential and Vee-based approaches dominate in regulated, safety-critical, and high-assurance domains, where a stable, documented, traceable specification is worth the loss of flexibility.

Incremental methods

Incremental approaches deliver an initial capability and then add to it through a planned series of deliveries. The full scope of capability is generally known at the outset, but development is split into increments to absorb technical risk, smooth resource demand, and allow newer technology to be inserted as it matures. A steady cadence between increments aids planning and builds stakeholder confidence, since each increment is a visible, working step rather than a promise. The Incremental Commitment Spiral Model (ICSM) is a well-known example: each turn of the spiral addresses requirements and solutions together, growing the level of detail and the team's understanding, and proceeds to the next turn only when the risks are judged acceptable at an evidence-based commitment review. The ICSM is therefore risk-driven — a programme earns the right to continue rather than simply reaching a date.

Incremental delivery is attractive when stakeholders want value early and the programme wants to retire risk in stages, but it depends on an architecture designed to be extended — adding the fourth increment should not require tearing apart the first three. Investing in clean interfaces and modularity up front is what keeps later increments cheap to add.

Evolutionary methods

When the final requirements are unknown or only partly known — common in novel systems, research and development, and systems-of-systems — an evolutionary approach provides the necessary adaptability. Cycles are planned at regular intervals, each producing a deployable version, and the requirements are progressively discovered and refined cycle by cycle as stakeholders react to something real. The defining difference from incremental work is that the scope is genuinely open at the start; the system is expected to evolve as the problem itself becomes clearer. Agile methods are the most widely used form of evolutionary development, prizing working increments, close stakeholder feedback, and responsiveness to change over heavy up-front specification — trading some predictability for the ability to learn and adapt.

The cost of this adaptability is reduced up-front predictability, so evolutionary methods rely on disciplined cadence, automated testing, and continuous stakeholder involvement to stay under control. Used well they converge on the right system through rapid feedback; used carelessly they can drift without ever stabilising, which is why even agile efforts keep lightweight gates and a clear definition of done.

DevSecOps, mixed approaches, and choosing

Modern software-intensive systems often blend continuous development with continuous operations. DevSecOps integrates security throughout a tight, automated develop-and-operate loop — plan, code, build, test, release, deploy, operate, monitor — shrinking the distance between a change and its safe deployment and treating security as a continuous concern rather than a final checkpoint. In practice, large programmes are usually mixed: a safety-critical hardware element may follow a Vee while its software follows an agile or DevSecOps cadence, all coordinated under one programme with synchronised increments. The handbook is emphatic that there is no universal best model. Selection depends on environment stability, risk, novelty and complexity, integrity needs (safety, security, privacy), available budget and enabling systems, and any required conformance to standards. The right question is not “which model is best?” but “which approach, or combination, fits this situation?” — and the answer can change as a programme matures and its uncertainties resolve.

A quick worked example: matching model to situation

Imagine three projects. A new implantable medical device has firm, regulated requirements and demands the highest assurance, so it leans toward a sequential, Vee-based approach with formal gates and audits — predictability and traceability matter more than speed. A large enterprise platform whose broad scope is known but whose technology will evolve over several years suits an incremental approach, delivering useful capability in planned increments while inserting newer technology along the way. A startup building a novel consumer app, where the real requirements will only emerge from user feedback, suits an evolutionary, agile cadence that discovers the product as it goes. Most real programmes are a blend: the medical device still uses agile cadences for its software, and the enterprise platform still runs a tight DevSecOps loop for its services. The point is not to pick a label but to read the situation — requirements stability, risk, integrity needs, and constraints — and assemble the approach that fits it.

From models to processes

If stages answer “in what order do we do the work?” and models answer “in what shape?”, then processes answer “what work, exactly?” A process is a defined set of activities and tasks performed to achieve an outcome for a stated purpose. ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (2023) provides a common framework of system life-cycle processes, and the INCOSE handbook describes each one with a consistent structure: purpose, description, inputs and outputs, activities, and common approaches, illustrated by an input–process–output (IPO) diagram. Two ideas in the IPO diagram are easy to miss but important: every process is governed by shared controls (laws, standards, agreements, plans, project direction) and supported by shared enablers (people, infrastructure, tools, and the quality, knowledge, configuration, and information systems). Because these apply to every process, they are drawn once and not repeated.

Reading the processes this way pays off quickly: each description names the inputs a process consumes, the outputs it produces, and the handful of activities that transform one into the other — typically a prepare step that plans the work and secures enabling systems, a perform step that does it, and a manage results step that records evidence and maintains traceability. Once you recognise that rhythm, any of the thirty processes becomes quick to read, and the relationships between them — which output feeds which input — become the real subject of study.

Concurrency, iteration, and recursion

The single biggest misconception about the 15288 processes is that they are a checklist run once in order. They are not. They are applied throughout the life cycle in three overlapping ways:

  • Concurrency — two or more processes running in parallel at the same level of the hierarchy, for example risk management and measurement operating continuously alongside the technical work and feeding one another.
  • Iteration — repeatedly cycling between processes at a level to absorb new decisions and understanding, for example moving back and forth between system requirements and architecture until they are mutually consistent.
  • Recursion — re-applying the same set of processes, suitably tailored, at each successive level of the system hierarchy, where the outputs at one level become the inputs at the next, lower level.

These three patterns are what allow one finite set of processes to scale to a system of almost any size and depth, from a single component to a system-of-systems.

The four process groups

The 15288 processes are organised into four groups. The first two set up the environment and the deal between parties; the last two manage and perform the engineering. The Detailed Review walks through every individual process; the goal here is to fix the shape of each group in mind.

Agreement processes

These establish an agreement between two parties for the acquisition or supply of a system, element, product, or service. They are the Acquisition process (obtaining a product or service in line with the acquirer's requirements) and the Supply process (providing the acquirer with a product or service that meets the agreed requirements). The two are mirror images of one transaction. Systems engineers contribute the impact assessments, trade studies, and risk assessments that make such agreements realistic, and clear, verifiable requirements and acceptance criteria at this stage prevent costly disputes later.

In an acquisition, the acquirer prepares a strategy and a request, selects a supplier against objective criteria, and then monitors the agreement all the way to acceptance; in a supply, the provider responds only to commitments it can meet and delivers against the agreed plan. The recurring lesson is that the agreement — its requirements and acceptance criteria — must be explicit and verifiable, because ambiguity here turns into dispute later.

Organizational project-enabling processes

These provide the organisational environment and resources that projects rely on, sitting above any single project. They are Life Cycle Model Management (defining and improving the organisation's processes and models), Infrastructure Management (facilities, tools, environments), Portfolio Management (choosing and funding the right projects to meet strategy), Human Resource Management (supplying and sustaining competencies), Quality Management (setting quality policy and objectives, and tying quality to culture), and Knowledge Management (capturing and re-applying knowledge). Together they decide which projects to run, supply the people, tools, and facilities, define the processes projects must follow, and ensure the organisation gets better over time rather than relearning the same lessons.

These processes are usually owned by the enterprise rather than by any one project, and they are what make repeatable engineering possible: a project should inherit proven processes, ready infrastructure, competent people, and reusable knowledge rather than inventing each from scratch. When they are weak, every project quietly pays the same start-up tax over and over again.

Technical management processes

These plan, assess, and control the technical effort — the management spine that keeps engineering aligned with cost, schedule, and risk. They are Project Planning, Project Assessment and Control, Decision Management, Risk Management, Configuration Management, Information Management, Measurement, and Quality Assurance. Several are deeply interlinked: measurement feeds assessment and control, which informs decisions; risk management surfaces the risks that measurement then tracks; configuration and information management keep everyone working from the same controlled baseline. Risk is characterised by likelihood and consequence together, and the same discipline applied to upsides becomes opportunity management.

A useful way to see this group is as a control loop wrapped around the engineering: planning sets the intent, measurement and assessment reveal reality, decision and risk management choose the response, and configuration and information management make sure everyone acts on the same, current information. Remove any one of them and the loop leaks.

Technical processes

These turn a need into a realised, operated, and eventually retired system — the heart of the engineering. They are Business or Mission Analysis; Stakeholder Needs and Requirements Definition; System Requirements Definition; System Architecture Definition; Design Definition; System Analysis; Implementation; Integration; Verification; Transition; Validation; Operation; Maintenance; and Disposal. Read in order they trace the familiar arc of the Vee: understand the problem, define the need, specify the system, architect and design it, build and integrate it, prove it is built right (verification) and that it is the right thing (validation), field it, sustain it, and finally retire it. In practice they run concurrently, iteratively, and recursively, not as a single sweep from top to bottom.

A practical anchor is the verification–validation pairing the right leg of the Vee makes concrete: build each element (implementation), combine them (integration), confirm each is built right (verification), field it (transition), and confirm the whole is the right thing in use (validation), before operating, maintaining, and finally disposing of it. Each of these is applied recursively at every level of the system, not just once for the system as a whole.

Verification versus validation

Two of the technical processes are so often confused that they deserve a line of their own. Verification asks “did we build the system right?” — does the product conform to its specified requirements and characteristics, checked by inspection, analysis, demonstration, or test. Validation asks “did we build the right system?” — does the system, when used by real users in its real environment, actually meet the business or mission objectives and stakeholder needs. A system can be perfectly verified against a flawed specification and still fail validation; both questions must be answered, and both are applied at every level of the hierarchy.

How stages, gates, models, and processes relate

It helps to keep the four ideas in their proper relationship. Stages are spans of time the system lives through, from concept to retirement. Decision gates are the checkpoints between and within stages where the project commits, holds, or stops, based on evidence. Models — sequential, incremental, evolutionary, and their blends — describe the shape in which the stages are executed. And processes are the actual activities, drawn from the four groups, applied within the stages and shaped by the chosen model. A common mistake is to equate one with another — to treat the “development stage” as if it were the “technical processes,” or to think choosing the Vee tells you which processes to run. In fact the same processes are used under any model; the model only changes how often and in what order they are applied, and the stages only change what the work is aiming to achieve at that point in time.

The thread that ties it together
Stages give the work a timeline; decision gates protect the timeline with evidence-based go/no-go choices; models shape how the stages are executed; and the four process groups supply the detailed, repeatable work — applied concurrently, iteratively, and recursively at every level of the system. Master that relationship and the rest of the handbook becomes a matter of detail.

Tailoring: the framework is a starting point

A final idea runs underneath the whole chapter: none of this is meant to be applied mechanically. The stages, gates, models, and processes form a comprehensive framework that organisations and projects tailor to their context — scaling the formality of gates, choosing and mixing models, and selecting and adapting processes to suit the system's risk, novelty, integrity needs, and contractual environment. A two-person research prototype and a national infrastructure programme will both recognise the same framework, yet apply it very differently. Tailoring well — keeping what manages real risk and trimming what does not — is itself a core systems-engineering skill, and getting it wrong in either direction (too heavy or too light) is a common cause of trouble.

Tailoring runs in both directions. Scaling up adds rigour — more formal gates, independent verification, deeper analysis — where safety, security, or scale demand it. Scaling down strips ceremony where a small, low-risk effort would only be slowed by it. The skill is to keep whatever genuinely manages risk and discard whatever merely produces paperwork, and to write the choice down so the team works to one deliberate, shared approach rather than thirty private interpretations of the standard.

Key takeaways

Chapter 2 rewards a few durable mental models. Think in whole of life, because the cheapest decisions have the largest downstream consequences. Treat gates as honest, evidence-based risk checkpoints rather than ceremonies, and tune their formality to the situation. Choose a life-cycle model to fit the requirements stability, risk, and integrity needs in front of you, and expect to mix approaches across a single programme. Keep verification and validation distinct. And internalise that the 15288 processes are not a one-pass checklist but a toolkit applied again and again, concurrently and recursively, across the hierarchy.
