Orientation: matching SE to its context

Chapters 2 and 3 told you what SE does. Chapter 4 is about fit — adapting that machinery to the situation in front of you. Keep four questions in mind. How much process? (§4.1 Tailoring.) Which way of working? (§4.2 Methodologies and approaches.) What kind of system? (§4.3 System types.) In which industry? (§4.4 Product sectors.) None of these change the fundamentals; they change the emphasis, sequence, vocabulary, and rigor with which the fundamentals are applied.

Exam focus — ASEP
For each topic, lock in (1) a one-line definition, (2) why it differs from the "default" greenfield/document-based/single-system view, and (3) one signature distinction — e.g., the tailoring risk-balance curve, MBSE vs. document-based, greenfield vs. brownfield, Maier's two principal SoS traits, and the four SoS types. The §4.4 sectors are recognition-level: know each domain's headline driver.

Tailoring considerations (§4.1)

Most standards "cannot be directly applied" to a given organization or project, so they are tailored: adapted to specific circumstances while being scaled to a level of rigor that keeps life-cycle activities at an acceptable level of risk. The guiding idea is balance — insufficient SE brings a high risk of technical issues and cost/schedule overruns, while excessive formal process also drives up cost; the goal is the "correct amount of tailoring." Tailoring is dynamic over the life cycle and must be continually monitored and adjusted.

The ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 Tailoring process adapts processes to circumstances surrounding an organization, influencing a project, or reflecting an organization's needs. Its activities: identify and record influencing circumstances; account for life-cycle structures mandated by standards; obtain input from affected parties; make tailoring decisions; and select and tailor the affected processes (tailoring can delete, modify, or add outcomes, activities, tasks, inputs, or outputs). Two levels matter: organizational tailoring (creating/maintaining the processes used by all projects, in concert with Life Cycle Model Management — understand the organization and the standard, adapt the standard to the organization, institutionalize at the "right" level, and allow tailoring) and project tailoring (adapting organizational processes to one project via Project Planning, driven by stakeholders, budget/schedule, risk tolerance, complexity/precedence, and integration needs).

Traps and very small enterprises

Common traps: reusing another system's tailored baseline without repeating tailoring; using all processes "just to be safe"; assuming a single set of measures/risks/controls fits every project; using a pre-established tailored baseline; and failing to include relevant stakeholders. For Very Small Enterprises (VSEs — up to 25 people, including small projects inside big organizations), the ISO/IEC/IEEE 29110 series provides four profiles — Entry (<6 people/start-ups), Basic (single application, single team), Intermediate (multiple parallel projects), and Advanced — though these do not apply to mission- or safety-critical projects, which demand greater rigor.

SE methodologies and approaches (§4.2)

The choice of methodology/approach, with the chosen life-cycle model, affects the sequence of work and how resources are focused. The handbook introduces four; others exist.

Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

MBSE is "the formalized application of modeling to support system requirements, design, analysis, verification, and validation activities beginning in the concept stage and continuing throughout development and later life-cycle stages" (SE Vision 2020). It is contrasted with the document-based approach, in which information scattered across specifications, ICDs, trade studies, and reports is hard to synchronize, maintain, and assess for quality. MBSE makes a system descriptive model (often expressed in SysML, augmented by geometric and analytical models) the primary artifact. Benefits with industry support: improved communication, managing complexity through multiple views, improved quality (unambiguous, checkable models), reduced cycle time, reduced risk (early surfacing of issues), enhanced knowledge capture/reuse, and easier teaching of SE. An MBSE methodology must itself be tailored. OOSEM (Object-Oriented Systems Engineering Method) is a representative method — consistent with this handbook, adaptable to different life-cycle models, flowing requirements down from mission to system to element, applied recursively through manage / specify-and-design / develop-elements / integrate-and-verify subprocesses.

Agile Systems Engineering

Knowledge of requirements often keeps changing — from insufficient initial knowledge, new knowledge during development, and continual evolution of the operational environment. Agile SE is a principle-based approach for designing, building, sustaining, and evolving systems under uncertainty or dynamic environments; it is a "what," not a "how." It is best understood against the SE life-cycle spectrum, whose extremes are "extremely sequential" (certain knowledge, static environment) and "extremely agile" (uncertain knowledge, dynamic environment); real approaches sit in between. The Agile SE life-cycle model places the six life-cycle stages around a perimeter with situational awareness at the centre, allowing stages to run concurrently without strict sequencing (distinct from the Vee model). Operationally it builds on the three nested systems of §1.3.4 (the engineered system, the life-cycle project-management system, and the enterprise process & innovation system) and on sensing/responding/evolving behaviours. Published examples (Rockwell Collins, US Navy SPAWAR, Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin) show the same fundamental strategies tailored to very different contexts.

Lean Systems Engineering

Studies show large waste in engineering projects (the LAI found ~88% of charged time was waste). Lean thinking (rooted in Toyota's just-in-time philosophy) focuses on delivering maximum customer value with minimal waste — "doing the right job right the first time." Lean SE is the synergy of lean thinking and SE; crucially it means better SE (more responsibility, authority, accountability), not less. Three fundamentals: value (in lean SE, mission assurance plus stakeholder satisfaction), waste (eight categories: overprocessing, waiting, unnecessary movement, overproduction, transportation, inventory, defects, and waste of human potential), and the process of creating value without waste, captured in six lean principles — value, value stream, flow, pull, perfection, and respect-for-people. The 2012 Lean Enablers for Managing Engineering Programs (PMI/INCOSE/LAI) added project-management enablers under ten challenge themes (firefighting, unstable requirements, weak coordination, locally-optimized processes, unclear accountability, culture/knowledge mismanagement, insufficient planning, improper metrics, weak risk management, poor acquisition/contracting).

Product Line Engineering (PLE)

Few organizations build just one variant of anything, so SE is often performed for a product line — a family of similar systems with feature variations (in SE terms, a "system family"). Early approaches (clone-and-own, branch-and-merge) create ever-growing duplicate, divergent effort and error-prone dissonance across disciplines — a self-inflicted complexity. Feature-based PLE (INCOSE PLE Primer; ISO/IEC 26580) engineers the family as a single holistic system, managing variation with one authoritative model. Its PLE Factory has five elements: a Feature Catalogue (the single source of truth about variation), Bills-of-Features (the features chosen for each member), Shared Asset Supersets (engineering artifacts with embedded variation points), a PLE Factory Configurator (automation applying a Bill-of-Features to each variation point), and Product Asset Instances (the per-system outputs). Engineers then work on the Factory, not on individual instances, and change/configuration management is done once on the Factory. The ROI — eliminating low-value replicative work — is typically compelling.

System types considerations (§4.3)

Greenfield and brownfield systems

Borrowing from real estate: greenfield ("clean sheet"/"blank slate") systems are new designs with no or limited legacy constraints — the way SE is traditionally taught, but rarely seen in pure form. Brownfield ("legacy"/"in-service") systems significantly modify, extend, or replace an existing as-is system in an existing environment, often carrying technical debt and explicit continuity requirements that force a deliberate transition. The two drive different life-cycle emphases across stage, focus, maturity, reviews, verification (whole SoI vs. only changed/impacted parts plus regression), validation, manufacturing, logistics, practices, and team composition. Brownfield SE augments the standard processes with site surveys and reconstruction to understand the as-is system, find gaps, and engineer the to-be system.

COTS-based systems

A key trade is "make vs. buy" — custom-built vs. outsourced/commercial-off-the-shelf. COTS is typically sold/leased/licensed to the public, vendor-supported and vendor-controlled, available in identical copies, and used without modification. Its promise (save time, reduce risk, faster/cheaper to market, latest technology) is often unrealized: COTS isn't built to your requirements, may have proprietary interfaces, may be poorly documented, raises trustworthiness and integration concerns, and carries obsolescence/DMSMS life-cycle-cost risk (IEC 62402 guides obsolescence management). Effective COTS use starts evaluation during needs analysis and introduces a new COTS integrator role and mindset.

Software-intensive systems

Per ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010, a software-intensive system is "any system where software contributes essential influences to the design, construction, deployment, and evolution of the system as a whole." Software (custom, reused/tailored, or licensed and often wrapped in shells) contributes functionality, behaviour, quality characteristics, interfaces, and performance. It is malleable and often cheaper/faster than hardware — but a physical element may be preferred where software cannot provide certifiable safety, security, or assurance. Making software-vs-physical trade-offs, partitioning requirements, allocating performance, and controlling physical-software interfaces are central challenges, complicated by differing software-engineering and SE cultures.

Cyber-physical systems (CPS)

CPS integrate physical and cyber (software) processes: software monitors and controls physical processes and is affected by them, enabled by sensors and feedback loops. Closely tied to Industry 4.0 and "smart" systems, CPS always combine software and hardware and are almost always networked (then they are cyber-physical systems of systems, CPSoS); if networked via Internet protocols they form part of an IoT. Two big engineering implications are complexity (large networks; nondeterministic combined physical/computational models) and ethics (transferring decisions from humans to machines). Because software pervades modern systems, SE practitioners very often deal with CPS and CPSoS.

Systems of systems (SoS)

An SoS (ISO/IEC/IEEE 21839) is "a set of systems or system elements that interact to provide a unique capability that none of the constituent systems can accomplish on its own." Each constituent is useful on its own, with its own development, goals, and resources. Maier's characteristics are operational independence, managerial independence, geographic distribution, emergent behaviour, and evolutionary development — the first two being the principal distinguishers. The four SoS types (ISO/IEC/IEEE 21841) by authority relationship are directed, acknowledged, collaborative, and virtual. Three standards (21839/21840/21841) give guidance, and Dahmann's SoS engineering challenges include authorities, leadership, constituent-system limitations, capabilities/requirements mismatch, autonomy/interdependence/emergence, testing, and a steep learning curve — plus distinctive security risks from asynchronously rearranged interfaces.

IoT / big-data-driven systems

SE rests on data, yet practitioners often decide by intuition. The 4th Industrial Revolution's sensors and big-data analytics let SE become more data-driven: bring diverse data and viewpoints, understand variation, deal with uncertainty, invest in high-quality trusted data, experiment, and revise decisions as new data arrives. The three goals of analyzing data are prediction, estimation, and explanation. Designing analytic IoT systems involves multi-stack layers and SoS/network-of-networks thinking, demanding interdisciplinary knowledge (computing, sensors/actuators, software, network, analytics, data science).

Service systems

A service (OASIS) is "a mechanism to enable access to one or more capabilities … using a prescribed interface … consistent with constraints and policies." A service system comprises service-system entities interacting through governance/management processes to cocreate value in a stakeholder context. Services have intangible attributes (quality of service, response time) and time-varying demand, and are rendered when requested — so design is about balancing resources against demand. Interoperability through interface agreements is paramount, and the SE practitioner acts as an integrator across technical, process, and organizational dimensions for the best customer experience.

Enterprise systems

Enterprise SE applies SE when the SoI is an enterprise — "a purposeful combination of interdependent resources (people, processes, organizations, technologies, funding) that interact … to achieve business and operational goals." An enterprise is not an organization: organizations participate in an enterprise and manage resources for it. An enterprise must both develop things (offerings or internal mechanisms) and transform itself to survive in a competitive environment. Individual competence contributes (through work practices) to organizational capability, which combines into system and operational capability delivering operational value. Enterprise SE addresses both problem-solving and opportunity exploitation, uses enterprise-architecture modelling to plan state changes, and assesses opportunities as a portfolio; governance shapes the political/operational/economic/technical landscape rather than tightly controlling it.

Application of SE by product sector (§4.4)

SE is generic, but how it is applied varies by domain. The handbook presents ten sectors alphabetically; the headline differentiators:

  • Automotive — diverse, high-volume, very competitive and standards-driven; complexity from electrification, connectivity, and autonomy; new ecosystems like Mobility-as-a-Service. Low margins make disruptive change hard.
  • Biomedical & Healthcare — "risk management" centres on patient safety rather than project risk; lean SE suits care-delivery improvement; device development follows quality-system standards (ISO 13485, ISO 14971, IEC 60601/62304/62366), with traceability key in audits.
  • Commercial Aerospace — complex and increasingly software-rich, often part of larger SoS; uses sequential, incremental, and agile models; standards like ARP 4754A; emphasis on interfaces, risk, and safety.
  • Defense — the home of modern SE; much of the handbook applies as-is with normal tailoring; emphasis on availability, harsh environments, long life cycles/logistics, HSI, and SoS interoperability (IEEE 15288.1 for US DoD).
  • Infrastructure — large, unique, on-site builds with multi-decade life; loosely-defined boundaries and evolving architectures; SE adds verifiable links between business needs and product (Table 4.7 maps SE terms to infrastructure terms).
  • Oil & Gas — relatively new SE adoption; biggest gains in requirements definition/management and digitalization; risk-averse, sequential stage-gate culture; energy-transition pressures.
  • Power & Energy — typically SoS-level, central to the sustainability transition; convergence of business, technology, and socio-politics; still relatively immature, needing cultural change.
  • Space — high reliability with little maintenance (it must work the first time); civil, commercial, and national-security segments with different risk postures and standards; HSI matters when crews are aboard.
  • Telecommunication — enablers for other services; COTS-heavy with industry interface standards; often critical infrastructure; SoS-like with multi-party ownership and long-lived constituents.
  • Transportation — long-lived (25–100+ years), publicly funded, in-service brownfield SoS; complexity from Intelligent Transportation Systems and smart cities; emerging SE practice (e.g., UK GRIP, ProRail).
