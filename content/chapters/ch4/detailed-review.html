4.0 Overview — tailoring and application considerations

Parts II and III of the handbook described the SE life-cycle processes and the cross-cutting analyses and methods. Chapter 4 turns to application: how to adapt SE to the methodologies, approaches, system types, product sectors, and domains in which it is actually practised. It has four sections — §4.1 Tailoring considerations, §4.2 SE methodology/approach considerations, §4.3 system types considerations, and §4.4 application of SE for specific product sectors or domains. The recurring theme is judgement: the fundamentals do not change, but their emphasis, sequence, vocabulary, and rigor are tuned to context.

4.1 Tailoring considerations

Many standards and handbooks address life-cycle models and SE processes, but in most cases they cannot be applied directly to a given organization or project — there is usually a need to tailor them for the specific project, organization, environment, or other situational factors. The principle is to adapt the processes so they meet organizational/project needs while being scaled to a level of rigor that lets the life-cycle activities be performed with an acceptable level of risk. In general all life-cycle processes can apply in all stages; tailoring determines the process level that applies to each stage, and processes are applied iteratively, recursively, and concurrently.

Tailoring occurs dynamically over the life cycle depending on risk and the situational environment, so it should be continually monitored and adjusted. The ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 Tailoring process exists to adapt the processes of the standard (and this handbook) to circumstances that surround an organization in an agreement, influence a project, or reflect an organization's needs.

The process activities are to identify and record the circumstances that influence tailoring (strategic/programmatic/technical risks, novelty/complexity, administrative effects such as geographic and organizational distribution and team size, and tailoring criteria for each stage); account for life-cycle structures recommended or mandated by standards; obtain input from affected parties (expertise/experience, stakeholder expectations, risk tolerance); make tailoring decisions (assess and record candidate life-cycle models); and select and tailor the affected processes, capturing rationale and a means to continuously evaluate the tailored processes. Tailoring can include the deletion, modification, or addition of outcomes, activities, tasks, inputs, or outputs.

Two levels of tailoring matter. Organizational tailoring creates and maintains the processes used by all projects (done with Life Cycle Model Management): understand the organization, understand the new standard, adapt the standard to the organization (not vice versa), institutionalize compliance at the "right" level, and allow for tailoring. Project tailoring adapts organizational processes to one project (done with Project Planning); influencing factors include stakeholders/acquirers, budget/schedule/requirements, risk tolerance, the complexity and precedence of the system, and the need for horizontal and vertical integration. Because complex systems are engineered by complex organizations (SE principle #12), harmony across organizations is best maintained by agreeing a consistent set of processes, methods, and tools.

Exam focus — tailoring traps
Know the common traps: (1) reusing a tailored baseline from another system without repeating the tailoring; (2) using all processes and activities "just to be safe"; (3) assuming a single set of measures/risks/controls applies to all projects; (4) using a pre-established tailored baseline; and (5) failing to include relevant stakeholders.

Very Small Enterprises (VSEs). The ISO/IEC/IEEE 29110 series defines VSEs as enterprises, organizations, departments, or projects with up to 25 people — a situation that can also arise inside a large organization running a small project. VSEs often struggle to apply international standards. The series derives four profiles from VSE characteristics: Entry (fewer than 6 people or start-ups), Basic (single application by a single team), Intermediate (more than one project in parallel with more than one team), and Advanced (to sustain and grow as an independent competitive developer). These do not apply to mission- or safety-critical projects, whose criticality demands far greater rigor.

4.2 SE methodology/approach considerations

System-definition activities — especially partitioning — require that integration is considered throughout development, and may need refining based on when and how work is performed. The choice of SE methodology/approach, with the chosen life-cycle model, affects the sequence of work and the focus of resources. The handbook introduces four methodologies/approaches; others exist.

4.2.1 Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE)

MBSE is "the formalized application of modeling to support system requirements, design, analysis, verification, and validation activities beginning in the concept stage and continuing throughout development and later life-cycle stages" (INCOSE SE Vision 2020). It is contrasted with a document-based approach, in which information lives in specifications, ICDs, system descriptions, trade studies, analysis reports, and verification plans/procedures/reports that are hard to synchronize, maintain, and assess for correctness, completeness, and consistency. A model-based approach is becoming essential to handle increasing system complexity and adapt to requirements and design changes.

Industry data (quantitative and largely qualitative) supports the following benefits of MBSE: improved communication among development stakeholders; increased ability to manage complexity through multiple perspectives; improved product quality via an unambiguous, precise model checkable for consistency/correctness/completeness; reduced cycle time (better baseline control, rapid impact analysis, reuse, early design insight, early defect discovery); reduced risk by surfacing requirements/design issues early; enhanced knowledge capture and reuse; and an improved ability to teach and learn SE.

In an MBSE approach much of the information traditionally captured in informal diagrams, text, and tables is captured in a system descriptive model (context, requirements, architecture structure and behaviour, critical parameters, and verification information), often using SysML, and augmented by geometric and analytical models. The descriptive model becomes the primary artifact of the SE process, and the various models must be managed together for a coherent representation. An MBSE methodology describes how MBSE is performed and must itself be tailored (life-cycle model, activities, work products, tools). Estefan's survey of candidate methodologies is catalogued on the INCOSE MBSE Wiki.

OOSEM (Object-Oriented Systems Engineering Method) is summarized as a representative method: it helps architect systems that satisfy evolving mission and system requirements and accommodate technology/design change, is generally consistent with this handbook, and can be adapted to different life-cycle models. It flows requirements down from mission to system to system-element levels and captures model-based artifacts in a SysML system descriptive model plus analytical models. Its subprocesses are manage the system development; specify and design the system; develop the system elements; and integrate and verify the system — applied recursively at each level of the hierarchy with multidisciplinary teams and a disciplined management process.

4.2.2 Agile Systems Engineering

Knowledge of requirements often keeps changing — from insufficient initial knowledge, new knowledge revealed during development and utilization, and continual evolution of the operational environment. When that environment never stops evolving, the system's capabilities must evolve to remain viable, so engineering is "virtually never ending" and retirement becomes safe/secure capability disposal rather than decommissioning. Agile Systems Engineering is a principle-based approach for designing, building, sustaining, and evolving systems when knowledge is uncertain or environments are dynamic — a "what," not a "how." Many life-cycle models exist (Vee, Incremental Commitment Spiral Model, DevSecOps; software-domain methods such as XP, Scrum, DevOps, and scaled approaches like SAFe), most focused on development.

An Agile SE process is based on strategies for timely, continual knowledge development and affordable application of new knowledge; virtually all forms use incremental or evolutionary development to produce demonstrable/usable work-in-process that provokes feedback for real-time learning. Software's growing role means agile software methods inform agile engineering in other domains — but each domain has unique differences (external dependencies, fabrication, cycle-time constraints, tools).

In this model, situational awareness has no entry/exit criteria (it is continuous); entry to other stages begins with a decision to act on triggering awareness, possibly with process-prudent or contract-required criteria. Because the process is predicated on real-time experimentation, entry may be as simple as deciding to enter a stage for experimental knowledge development, while exit criteria for stages that produce artifacts used elsewhere should have some fixed completion requirements. The retirement stage handles frequently-retired elements and older versions as the system evolves. Fleshing out a generic Agile SE model for a project starts from default standard processes in each stage, tailored and augmented for agile differences.

Operational considerations build on the three nested systems of §1.3.4: System 1, the engineered system; System 2, the life-cycle project-management system that produces System 1; and System 3, the enterprise process & innovation system that learns, configures, and matures System 2 and provides operational agility through continuous information flow. Operational agility comes from sensing (external/internal awareness, sense-making), responding (decision-making, action-making, action evaluation), and evolving (experimentation, evaluation, memory). The architecture/structural principles that enable system agility are covered in §3.1.3.

Four published examples illustrate tailoring the same fundamental strategies to different contexts: Rockwell Collins (a PLE radio-product family on a continuous-integration platform); the US Navy SPAWAR (off-road autonomous vehicle technology in six-month increments with parallel integration/test/architecture tracks); Northrop Grumman (a logistics software systems-of-systems hub evolved in six-month increments across three live generations); and Lockheed Martin (F-16/F-22/F-35 weapons capabilities evolved in roughly six-month increments in a tailored SAFe approach with a continuous integration/demonstration platform).

4.2.3 Lean Systems Engineering

SE is sound practice but is not always delivered efficiently — GAO and NASA studies document major budget/schedule overruns, and MIT's Lean Advancement Initiative found significant waste in government projects (averaging 88% of charged time). Lean thinking, rooted in Toyota's just-in-time philosophy, aims at "producing quality products efficiently through the complete elimination of waste, inconsistencies, and unreasonable requirements." Lean SE applies lean thinking to SE to deliver the best life-cycle value for technically complex systems with minimal waste. Importantly, lean SE does not mean less SE — it means better SE with higher responsibility, authority, and accountability, producing waste-free workflows with increased mission assurance. Three concepts are fundamental: value, waste, and the process of creating value without waste.

Value in lean SE is defined simply as mission assurance — delivering a flawless complex system with flawless technical performance — plus satisfying the customer and all stakeholders, implying minimal waste and cost and the shortest possible schedule. Waste is "the work element that adds no value to the product or service in the eyes of the customer"; the LAI's categories (seven, with an eighth commonly added) are overprocessing, waiting, unnecessary movement, overproduction, transportation, inventory, defects, and the waste of human potential. The process of creating value without waste is captured in six lean principles: the value principle (establish customer value with clarity early), the value stream principle (detailed planning, waste prevention, frontloading, healthy stakeholder relationships), the flow principle (uninterrupted first-time-right work, concurrency, visible progress), the pull principle (pull tasks and outputs from real customer needs to guard against rework/overproduction), the perfection principle (standardization, continuous improvement, lessons learned, real-time visibility of imperfections), and the respect-for-people principle (a culture of trust, openness, empowerment, and teamwork). The 2012 Lean Enablers for Managing Engineering Programs (PMI/INCOSE/LAI) added project-management enablers and analysed challenges under ten themes: firefighting, unstable/unclear/incomplete requirements, insufficient enterprise alignment/coordination, locally-optimized non-integrated processes, unclear roles/accountability, mismanagement of culture/competency/knowledge, insufficient planning, improper metrics/KPIs, lack of proactive risk management, and poor acquisition/contracting.

4.2.4 Product Line Engineering (PLE)

Rarely does anyone build just one edition of anything — SE is often performed for a product line, a family of similar systems with feature/function variations. (In SE terms: "system," "system family," and "system family engineering" — used interchangeably with the PLE field's "product" terms.) When family members are engineered as individual point solutions, clone-and-own or branch-and-merge reuse produces ever-growing duplicate and divergent effort, while managing commonality/variability via tribal knowledge and ad hoc per-discipline techniques creates error-prone dissonance across the life cycle — a self-inflicted complexity that consumes teams with low-value replicative work.

Feature-based PLE (INCOSE PLE Primer 2019; ISO/IEC 26580 2021) is the modern good practice: engineer the family as a single holistic system, consolidating each discipline's assets to eliminate duplication/divergence and applying one authoritative variation-management model across all assets and disciplines, enabling deliberate cost-benefit decisions about variation.

The PLE Factory has five key elements: the Feature Catalogue (a formal model of how family members differ — the single authoritative source of truth about variation); the Bill-of-Features (the features selected for each system in the portfolio); the Shared Asset Supersets (engineering artifacts containing a single copy of all content, with common content plus variation points for content that varies); the PLE Factory Configurator (automation that applies a Bill-of-Features to each variation point); and the Product Asset Instances (each containing only the content suited to one system). Variation-point mechanisms include selection/omission, mutually-exclusive alternatives, generation from feature specs, and feature-based transformation. Engineers then work on the Factory — Shared Asset Supersets, the Feature Catalogue, and Bills-of-Features — rather than on individual instances, which are automatically instantiated; change and configuration management are performed once on the Factory. Supersets can be built for requirements, MBSE/SysML models, electronic/mechanical/CAD designs, source code, V&V tests, and a broad array of other digital assets, and feature-based PLE applies to all SoI types in §4.3. The ROI — eliminating low-value replicative work, with multiple-fold improvements in complexity, time, cost, scalability, and quality — generally makes the organizational change compelling.

4.3 System types considerations

The concept of the system-of-interest (SoI) was introduced in §1.3.1; the type of SoI has significant implications for SE. This section covers nine types (others exist).

4.3.1 Greenfield / clean-sheet systems

"Greenfield" and "brownfield" are real-estate terms: greenfield land is previously undeveloped, brownfield land has existing structures and may contain hazardous "waste" needing remediation. Greenfield SE ("clean sheet"/"blank slate") involves new designs with no or limited legacy constraints other than system interfaces. It is how SE is traditionally taught — start from a blank sheet, elicit stakeholders and needs, derive system requirements, architect and design, implement elements, then integrate, verify, and validate. But few efforts are truly greenfield, so it is almost a theoretical situation; with today's incremental/spiral approaches a greenfield system may drift toward brownfield even before delivery. Organizations long accustomed to brownfield updates can find it traumatic to "relearn" greenfield development.

4.3.2 Brownfield / legacy systems

Brownfield SE ("legacy" SE) involves significant modification, extension, or replacement of an existing "as-is" system in an existing environment, producing an updated "to-be" system. Brownfield systems often carry waste (e.g., technical debt) needing remediation, and "in-service" systems are a common example. They typically have explicit continuity requirements — the as-is system must keep operating — driving a deliberate transition. Brownfield SE augments the handbook's processes with site surveys and reconstruction activities to understand the as-is systems, identify gaps, and engineer the to-be system.

4.3.3 Commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS)-based systems

A key trade study is the "make vs. buy" decision on system elements — "make" being custom-built, "buy" being outsourced development and COTS. Most systems have some COTS content. Useful COTS characteristics: sold/leased/licensed to the general public; offered by a profit-seeking vendor who retains the IP; vendor-controlled maintenance and update frequency; available in multiple identical copies; and used without hardware or source-code modification. COTS promises to save development time, reduce technical risk, and cut time-to-market and cost-to-market while exploiting the latest technology — but these promises are often unrealized. Considerations include: COTS not built to your requirements (missing/extra functionality, unwanted behaviours); unique or proprietary interfaces; vendor claims/decisions affecting schedule; limited insight into safe/secure operation and trustworthiness (open-source, third-party software); insufficient documentation; "Not Invented Here" resistance; missed delivery times; special integration challenges; lack of verification data for your environment; "sole source" supplier risk; and the need to consider whole life-cycle cost given obsolescence and DMSMS (IEC 62402 guides obsolescence management). Effective COTS use generally requires evaluation starting during needs analysis, sometimes an "internal sales pitch" per candidate, and a new COTS integrator role with skills different from "make" development.

4.3.4 Software-intensive systems

ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 (2022) defines a software-intensive system as "any system where software contributes essential influences to the design, construction, deployment, and evolution of the system as a whole." Software is encapsulated in system elements and contributes functionality, behaviour, quality characteristics, interfaces, and performance indicators. It may be custom-built, reused from libraries (and tailored), or licensed (usually unmodifiable, often wrapped in shells/wrappers that expose needed capabilities while masking unwanted ones). Software provides "essential influences" because it is malleable and can often be constructed, modified, or procured faster and cheaper than equivalent physical elements — and can provide capabilities hard to realize in hardware. But sometimes a physical element is preferred where software cannot deliver certifiable safety, security, or performance at the necessary assurance levels, so making software-vs-physical trade-offs is essential and best done with physical and software engineers collaborating.

Software interfaces can be passive pass-throughs or actively coordinate interactions; they may be internal (among physical elements, among software elements, or between them) or external (to the physical environment, humans, and other systems). A "software-only" system is an application on a stable computing platform (often commodity hardware/software). Sometimes software's essential influence is observable at external interfaces (e.g., user-interface displays) and sometimes not (then the software is "embedded"). Software also enables interfaces among the systems of an SoS. Key SE challenges include partitioning requirements/elements, allocating performance to physical vs. software elements, establishing and controlling physical-software interfaces, and integrating them — challenges sharpened by the differing cultures, terminology, processes, and practices of SE and software engineering (see §5.3.1).

4.3.5 Cyber-physical systems (CPS)

CPS are the integration of physical and cyber (software) processes in which software monitors and controls the physical processes and is, in turn, affected by them — enabled by sensors and feedback loops whose prevalence has grown with cheaper, better sensor technology.

The algorithms control the dynamic behaviour of the CPS toward goals that may include homeostasis (maintaining equilibrium) — e.g., an automobile sensing an obstacle and braking/steering, or monitoring brake-pad wear to prompt maintenance. Digital twins — dynamic, physics-based digital surrogates of physical assets — are an important related concept (see §4.3.7). CPS are closely aligned with Industry 4.0 ("smart" systems) and always include both software and hardware; they are almost always networked, in which case they are cyber-physical systems of systems (CPSoS). If networked using Internet protocols they form part of an IoT (though they may interact mechanically, electromagnetically, or thermally instead).

CPS feature in almost every industry — from robotic manufacturing and smart cities to automated insulin delivery and critical-infrastructure control — and can underpin business models by providing resilience or cost savings. Two of the most significant engineering implications are complexity (extensive networks; combined physical-plus-computational models that are nearly always nondeterministic due to a lack of temporal semantics, per Lee 2015) and ethics (transferring decision-making from humans to machines). The CPS Concept Map (Asare et al.) frames CPS as feedback systems applicable wherever a dynamic system is controlled by software, highlighting explicit needs for security, safety, and V&V, and pointing toward MBSE-based tool chains. Because software is integral to so many systems, SE practitioners very often deal with CPS and usually CPSoS.

4.3.6 Systems of systems (SoS)

ISO/IEC/IEEE 21839 (2019) defines a System of Systems as "a set of systems or system elements that interact to provide a unique capability that none of the constituent systems can accomplish on its own." Each constituent is a useful system in its own right — with its own development, management goals, and resources — but interacts within the SoS to provide the unique capability. Maier's (1998) characteristics for recognising an SoS are operational independence and managerial independence of the constituents (the two principal distinguishers), plus geographic distribution, emergent behaviour, and evolutionary development.

Three standards guide SoS work: ISO/IEC/IEEE 21839 (SoS considerations across a system's life-cycle stages), 21840 (applying ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 in an SoS context), and 21841 (a taxonomy of SoS types by authority relationship), summarised below.

Dahmann (2014) identified SoS engineering challenges: authorities (each constituent has its own owner; no single overarching authority); leadership (coherence through influence and incentives rather than control); constituent systems (often in-service systems built for other purposes, with limited ability to support the SoS); capabilities and requirements (SoS needs may not match constituent requirements, requiring changes or added systems with the SoS acting as "user"); autonomy, interdependence, and emergence (independent change and interdependencies create unpredictable SoS-level behaviour); testing (independent, asynchronously-evolving, large/diverse constituents make traditional end-to-end testing difficult or impossible, so performance is often gauged from operations or modelling); and SoS principles (a steep learning curve and limited knowledge transfer). Security adds a distinctive challenge: vulnerabilities can emerge from the overall SoS configuration even when individual constituents are secure. Approaches that combine systematic methods with holistic, nonlinear, iterative ones are growing — e.g., the SoS Wave Model and DANSE.

4.3.7 Internet of Things (IoT) / big-data-driven systems

SE rests on data — requirements, calculations, testing, modelling, and simulation — yet practitioners often decide by intuition or qualitative judgement. The 4th Industrial Revolution's sensor proliferation and big-data analytics create an opportunity for SE to become more data-driven. Recommended practices: bring diverse data and viewpoints to maximize information quality; use data to understand the business context and problem; appreciate the impact of variation; deal with uncertainty (and recognize mistakes); invest in high-quality, trusted data; conduct good experiments and research; recognize and adapt decision criteria; treat a decision as the first step and revise it as new data warrants; bring new data technologies into the organization; apply lessons-learned; and be a role model for data-driven work. The three goals of analyzing data are prediction, estimation, and explanation; predictive modelling forecasts new observations while explanatory models address causality. Designing analytic IoT systems involves "multi-stack" layers and SoS/network-of-networks thinking, so the practitioner needs interdisciplinary knowledge of computing, sensors/actuators, software, networks, analytics, and data science.

4.3.8 Service systems

OASIS (2012) defines a service as "a mechanism to enable access to one or more capabilities, where the access is provided using a prescribed interface and is exercised consistently with constraints and policies as specified by the service description." It applies specialized competences through deeds, processes, and performances for the benefit of another entity or itself; the entity may be technical, socio-technical, or strictly social.

Services not only produce value through provider-consumer interaction but have intangible attributes such as quality of service (e.g., ambulance availability, emergency response time). Demand varies by time of day, week, and season (and unexpected events), and services are rendered when requested — so design and operation are "all about finding the appropriate balance between the resources devoted to the system and the demands placed on it" (Daskin). A service-SE approach considers five resource types — people; tangible products and environment/infrastructure; organizations and institutions; protocols; and shared information and symbolic knowledge. Major challenges include the dynamic, adapting nature of service systems and breaking down knowledge silos. Interoperability of service-system entities through interface agreements must be at the forefront, and the practitioner must act as an integrator — addressing technical integration plus the processes and organization needed for an optimal customer experience and service innovation.

4.3.9 Enterprise systems

Enterprise SE applies SE principles when the SoI is an enterprise, aiming to continuously improve and transform the enterprise to deliver value and survive in a competitive environment. An enterprise is "a purposeful combination (e.g., a network) of interdependent resources (people, processes, organizations, supporting technologies, and funding) that interact … to achieve business and operational goals through a complex web of interactions distributed across geography and time." An enterprise must do two things: develop things (external offerings or internal mechanisms) and transform itself to perform more effectively and survive. Critically, an enterprise is not equivalent to an "organization": organizations participate in an enterprise but are not necessarily "part" of it.

An enterprise creates value for society, other stakeholders, and its participating organizations. Many organizational forms implement value-creating enterprises (companies, networks, programs/projects, virtual organizations); a typical business participates in multiple enterprises through its project portfolio, and a large SE project can itself be an enterprise. Because environments change, an enterprise must constantly adapt its capabilities to meet strategic goals.

The word "capability" means "the ability to do something useful under a particular set of conditions." Three kinds are distinguished — organizational, system, and operational capability — while "competence" refers to people's ability relative to the SE task (individual competence contributes to, but does not solely determine, organizational capability). New systems with new/enhanced capabilities are developed to enhance enterprise operational capability in response to stakeholder concerns. Enterprise SE addresses more than problem-solving: it also exploits opportunities (lowering operating costs, increasing market share, decreasing deployment risk, reducing time to market) and works with both "hard" elements (hardware, software) and "soft" ones (people, processes, policies, doctrines, beliefs).

An enterprise must consider its own enabling assets (personnel, facilities, networks, computing, policies, tools, funding, partnerships, equipment) when addressing stakeholder needs; their purpose is to effect state changes toward targeted performance. Because of high complexity, formalized enterprise-architecture analysis is advisable to model states and the impact of each enabling asset, determining where elements should be added, dropped, or changed. The identified modifications represent opportunities (and sometimes "divestment" — selling assets, reducing capacity, cancelling projects); because opportunities have dependencies, they are usually assessed as a portfolio via business-case assessment and portfolio-management techniques. Results feed an enterprise plan (sometimes an enterprise ConOps) covering a long horizon (often more than a decade), aligned with strategic goals and leadership priorities. Practical good practices (Rebovich and White): set enterprise fitness as the key measure of success (leveraging game theory and ecology); deal with uncertainty and conflict through adaptation (variety, selection, exploration, experimentation); and use layered architectures with loose couplers — governing rather than tightly controlling the enterprise.

4.4 Application of SE for specific product sectors or domains

SE is a generic discipline applicable in most situations and domains (with varying value), but the details of how it is applied vary. The ten domains below are presented alphabetically; the list is not exhaustive.

4.4.1 Automotive systems

The automotive industry has a long history of engineering increasingly complex consumer products — diverse ranges, high volumes, intense competition — balancing mass-production efficiency with driving performance through strong, well-orchestrated supply-chain processes. Electrification, connectivity, autonomy, and consumer choice are driving complexity and bringing SE into the mainstream (see the INCOSE Automotive SE Vision 2025). SE emerged in automotive as on-board electronics and software grew from the 1970s (engine control, ABS, climate control), through the 1990s electronic-control-unit networks, and via investment in stakeholder-needs and electrical/electronic architecture work. Contemporary practice varies widely — some requirements-led, some architecture-centric, with the system boundary drawn at vehicle, feature, element, or domain level — and has entered the industry incrementally because low-margin business models rely on trusted, repeatable, reuse-heavy processes and just-in-time high-volume production. Full autonomy and high-integrity connected features make complexity and cybersecurity management a great challenge, while incumbents face competition from software-intensive newcomers. Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS) is an emerging ecosystem — effectively an SoS of public and private mobility operators (e.g., in Paris, cars fell from 46% of trips in 2002 to 13% in 2022). The future points to highly configurable software platforms, compressed development cycles, service-centric architectures, and security-informed safety paradigms.

4.4.2 Biomedical and healthcare systems

SE has become more important to healthcare as systems and processes grow more complex and quality characteristics (safety, security, reliability, HSI) become more challenging. Benefits include well-defined processes/standards for design and development; open-standards architectures for interfacing, networking, and communications; scaling without compromising quality; better insight and control over production systems; and augmented user experience via digital platforms (AR/VR, robotics). A key distinction: in the medical-device world "risk management" generally means product (user safety) risk — "system safety" in this handbook (§3.1.11) — rather than project (technical/business) risk (§2.3.4.4). SE in healthcare delivery differs markedly from defense/aerospace/automotive: most projects improve an imperfect workflow or design a limited-scope new one in a clinic, hospital, lab, or population-health setting, with only a few stakeholders and requirements; lean SE works well, and methods like SEIPS tailor SE to patient-centered medicine. For medical devices, some companies use more traditional SE while tailoring to demonstrate value; standards such as ISO 13485, ISO 14971 (risk management), IEC 60601 (safety), IEC 62304 (software life cycle), and IEC 62366 (usability) drive deeper attention to system safety, and the sector increasingly needs SoS management, stakeholder management, agile development, trade analysis, MBSE, and PLE. Across the domain, patient-safety risk and traceability (key in regulatory submissions/audits) are emphasised.

4.4.3 Commercial aerospace systems

SE is part of the strategy for developing commercial aerospace solutions, whose complexity continues to increase as software enables more functions (with the expectation of faster availability than historic mechanical systems). Commercial aerospace systems are often part of larger SoS, and future systems will add autonomy, AI/neural networks, novel propulsion, advanced HSI, and cybersecurity. They use sequential, incremental, and evolutionary models (including agile with smaller cycles). Organizations have specific policies, standards, and guidebooks — e.g., ARP 4754A for verifying commercial-aircraft requirements — and the domain spans systems well beyond the aircraft (such as air-traffic-control). New applications (flying cars, unmanned/autonomous vehicles) demand understanding of new operational ecosystems and user lifestyles. SE helps through stakeholder management (from governments to passengers), model-based design maintaining requirements-to-model traceability, rigorous interface management (elements built worldwide and assembled centrally), risk management (especially for new technologies), and safety (ensuring organizational factors do not compromise it). It serves OEMs (design, manufacture, assembly, integration testing; scaling production; congestion/traffic insight; richer cockpits) and airlines (fleet maintenance, balancing performance and environmental impact, managing services).

4.4.4 Defense systems

Modern SE has its roots in twentieth-century defense systems, becoming a distinct activity in the late 1950s–early 1960s as system complexity, integration challenges, and the introduction of digital computers and software grew. Defense SE evolved to address widespread COTS adaptation and SoS approaches, providing designs/architectures, processes, and roadmaps for interfacing, networking, and communications that improve integrity and interoperability of real-time intelligence data across vendors and platforms via open standards — with increasing emphasis on end-to-end SoS performance and integration. Unique considerations: complex technical systems with many stakeholders and compressed timelines; high availability in extreme worldwide conditions (deserts to arctic); long life cycles (logistics is paramount); strong human interaction (usability/HSI is critical); and occasional needs to accelerate development/production for emergencies. Much of the handbook's processes apply as-is with normal project tailoring, but care is needed to reassert a defense focus now that ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 is domain- and country-neutral; IEEE 15288.1 (2014) provides a US DoD implementation basis allowing flexible innovation and tailoring.

4.4.5 Infrastructure systems

This domain covers physical capital projects — public works, transport, complex buildings, industrial facilities. SE practices are most developed in high-technology elements (software, control systems, security, safety); infrastructure projects often define the high-level solution without requirements decomposition, allocation, or interface identification, with architectures, traceability, and relationships implied rather than specified. Owners benefit by applying SE to create systematic, verifiable links between business needs and the final product. Infrastructure differs from manufacturing: large, unique systems built on-site, adapted to existing environments, with loosely-defined boundaries, evolving architectures, multi-phase implementation spanning decades, and multi-decade asset life cycles — so expectations and solutions evolve over extended timeframes, most projects cannot be standardized, and there is usually no prototype. Many handbook processes apply, sometimes with different terminology (Table 4.7). SE helps with diverse stakeholders (governments, agencies, landowners, the public — making requirements/cost/schedule volatile), design-and-construction practices (discipline-specific codes; design dissociated from construction specs limits traceability), external/uncontrollable interfaces, and risk (contractual liability/commercial risk). Four processes are especially useful to introduce: requirements management, interface management, verification, and validation.

4.4.6 Oil and gas systems

SE adoption in oil & gas (O&G) is relatively new — most applications within the past decade — driven by oil-price fluctuation, increasingly complex systems, and emissions-reduction efforts that pushed a risk-averse, document-centric culture to challenge assumptions. The greatest need has been in system requirements definition and management: with a domain-wide focus on digitalization, leaders have moved to data-centric requirement sets, and INCOSE–API trials (2017–18) improved requirement quality and clearly separated instruction, information, and verification. Beyond requirements, SE practitioners develop system architectures, systematically define interfaces, apply requirements/configuration/change management tools, and use systems-thinking tools (context diagrams, functional trees) as a foundation for functional modelling and V&V; digitalization has even enabled auto-creation of specifications and test plans. Challenges include the difficulty of treating the long, segmented global supply chain as one domain, the energy transition toward renewables and net-zero targets (by 2050 for many companies), and uneven "pockets" of SE maturity within a well-established sequential (waterfall) stage-gate culture — so SE is often introduced only where a clear case for change exists, with new product development a frequent entry point.

4.4.7 Power and energy systems

The global energy system faces a complex set of requirements from the Paris Agreement, the UN Sustainable Development Goals, emissions reduction, and the need to avoid degrading social foundations and ecological ceilings — demanding many new or modified systems supported by a holistic systems approach (the UK Council for Science and Technology endorsed SE principles for Net Zero planning). The sustainability transition sits at the convergence of business, technology, and socio-politics, which SE can enable — yet incumbent organizations and legacy approaches resist change, so SE application remains relatively immature compared with defense, space, and transportation, needing a cultural shift toward shared knowledge management, portfolio management, and organizational infrastructure. SE heuristics translate well (an SoS perfectly describes distributed energy resources). SE helps through architecture/design (integrity and interoperability across upstream, mid-stream, and downstream infrastructure), risk handling (energy security and resilience), portfolio management (smart grids, district heating, renewables, demand-side response, EVs), and sustainability (whole-life emissions reductions). Power and energy systems are typically at the SoS level, must remain persistently operational at constant supply-demand equilibrium for billions of users, and have life cycles of 30–100 years (or thousands for nuclear decommissioning/waste) — requiring social science, systems science, and systems thinking, HSI feedback to align micro-behaviour with macro-stability, and attention to market dynamics and political levers, while avoiding technology that delivers a poor user experience.

4.4.8 Space systems

Space systems operate in the space environment and may include spacecraft (with payloads/instruments), mission packages, ground stations, data links, launch systems, and supporting infrastructure. Because deploying assets to orbit (or beyond) is costly, they typically require high reliability with little maintenance (other than software changes) — everything must work the first time or be compensated by operational workarounds, which shapes the risk posture. The domain has three overlapping areas with different motivations: Civil (government-acquired; performance-risk focus; sequential Vee/waterfall), Commercial (profitability-driven; more amenable to incremental/evolutionary approaches for faster deployment and learning), and National Security Space (may emphasise performance over cost/schedule; traditional Vee/waterfall; more tolerant of new-technology risk). Key SE emphases are integration, verification (including testing), and validation of highly reliable, well-characterized systems, plus risk management for technology insertion. Most space-faring nations and consortiums have their own standards (US Mil-Standards, NASA and AIAA standards; the European ECSS; GOST under the Euro-Asian council; JAXA's JERG; plus ISO and IEEE), and human spaceflight adds HSI challenges with nation-specific standards (Roscosmos, NASA Human Space Flight Requirements, CNSA), with the US also governing commercial human spaceflight via the FAA.

4.4.9 Telecommunication systems

Telecommunication systems provide a route to transfer information across distinct endpoints, differing from postal systems in carrying electronic media rather than physical packages. They are enablers for other services — almost all modern systems use or contain telecommunication technologies (digital signage, battlespace communications, environmental monitoring) — and lives and livelihoods depend on them. As network complexity and the social cost of failures rise, applying and extending the best telecommunication SE approaches becomes opportune. The systems are built on diverse technologies (satellite, cellular, land mobile radio, microwave, radio/TV, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, GPS) and are increasingly software-intensive, spanning carrier, ISP, government, and broadcast networks plus over-the-top applications; some (the Internet, the public switched telephone network) have no single owner and depend on international standards collaboration. Typical characteristics: diverse geographic distribution; multi-party ownership/management; multiple constituent systems with independent, decades-long life cycles; a small stable set of functions; many node types; and strong interdependence between nodes. Engineering must be flexible enough to accommodate externally-owned network parts, depends on thorough failure-mode analysis, and faces significant configuration-management challenges. Telecom networks typically integrate COTS vendor equipment using industry interface standards and semi-standardized architectures, often without SE — which can produce unexpected outcomes; without SE-based disciplines, the risks are hard to assess or manage.

4.4.10 Transportation systems

Ground transportation (highways, buses, people-movers, mass transit, rail) involves complex capital programs for fleet acquisition and infrastructure and is invariably part of an SoS at the operational level. Asset life cycles run 25 to 100+ years, often with public funding and a related public trust, and operational processes are continuously optimized through block changes. The segment is an emerging SE practice area (transit authorities, railroad operators, rolling-stock manufacturers, civil-engineering firms); some regions advance faster, creating SoS approaches aligned with smart cities, an embedded safety culture, and through-life service delivery (e.g., the UK Institution of Civil Engineers), while others are at early stages — imbalances overcome over time through global collaboration. Rising complexity from Intelligent Transportation Systems and smart-city initiatives (train-control automation, bus scheduling, ride-share coordination, autonomous vehicles, micro-grid hybrid traction power, fare collection, trip-planner apps) mandates SE methods and competencies. Ground transportation services are typically in-service brownfield systems that must migrate to updated versions while maintaining service (some assets run 24/7), needing a "whole organization" approach. The domain shares infrastructure terminology (Table 4.7); early SE initiatives include the UK's Network Rail GRIP, EU open architectures (EULYNX, the Reference CCS Architecture), and the Netherlands' ProRail SE handbook.
