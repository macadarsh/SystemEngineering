What this chapter covers

Chapter 4 leaves the "what" of the life-cycle processes behind and turns to the "how, when, and where" — how to adapt Systems Engineering to real situations. It has four halves. §4.1 Tailoring adapts the standards and processes to an organization or project, scaling rigor to risk. §4.2 SE methodologies/approaches introduces four ways of working — MBSE, Agile SE, Lean SE, and Product Line Engineering. §4.3 System types walks through nine kinds of system-of-interest (greenfield, brownfield, COTS-based, software-intensive, cyber-physical, system of systems, IoT/big-data, service, and enterprise systems). §4.4 Product sectors shows how SE is practised across ten domains, from automotive to transportation.

Exam focus — ASEP
Chapter 4 is about matching SE to context. High-yield items: the tailoring risk-balance idea (too little or too much process both raise risk) and its traps; the four methodologies and what each is for; greenfield vs. brownfield; the COTS definition and "make vs. buy"; Maier's SoS characteristics and the four SoS types (directed, acknowledged, collaborative, virtual); and the relationship between CPS, SoS, and IoT. The product-sector subsections are descriptive — know the headline differentiators, not every detail.

Tailoring (§4.1)

Standards and handbooks rarely apply as-is, so they are tailored — adapted to the project, organization, and environment while scaling rigor to an acceptable level of risk. The key picture: too little SE invites technical, cost, and schedule overruns, but too much formal process also raises cost — there is a "correct amount of tailoring." Tailoring happens at the organization level (adapting external standards into organizational processes, via Life Cycle Model Management) and the project level (adapting organizational processes to one project, via Project Planning). It is dynamic across the life cycle and must be monitored and adjusted. The ISO/IEC/IEEE 29110 series helps Very Small Enterprises (VSEs, ≤25 people) with four profiles (Entry, Basic, Intermediate, Advanced), though not for safety/mission-critical work. Common traps: reusing a tailored baseline without re-tailoring, keeping "all processes just to be safe," assuming one set of controls fits all projects, and forgetting relevant stakeholders.

SE methodologies and approaches (§4.2)

  • Model-Based SE (MBSE) — the formalized application of modeling across the life cycle, contrasted with the document-based approach. The system descriptive model (often in SysML) becomes the primary artifact. Benefits: better communication, managing complexity, quality, shorter cycle time, lower risk, knowledge capture. OOSEM is a representative method.
  • Agile SE — a principle-based approach (a "what," not a "how") for evolving systems when knowledge is uncertain or environments are dynamic; understood against the SE life-cycle spectrum from extremely sequential to extremely agile. It relies on continual knowledge development and incremental/evolutionary work, with situational awareness at its centre.
  • Lean SE — applies lean thinking (Toyota roots) to SE: maximize value (defined as mission assurance), eliminate waste (8 categories), and follow the six lean principles (value, value stream, flow, pull, perfection, respect for people). Lean SE means better SE, not less.
  • Product Line Engineering (PLE) — engineers a family of systems as one. Feature-based PLE uses a PLE Factory: a Feature Catalogue, Bills-of-Features, Shared Asset Supersets with variation points, a Configurator, and Product Asset Instances — replacing error-prone clone-and-own with compelling ROI.

System types (§4.3)

The type of system-of-interest (SoI) strongly shapes SE. Greenfield ("clean sheet") systems are new designs with little legacy constraint — how SE is traditionally taught, but rare in practice. Brownfield ("legacy"/"in-service") systems modify an existing as-is system to a to-be system, with continuity requirements and technical debt (Table 4.1 contrasts the two). COTS-based systems hinge on the "make vs. buy" decision; COTS promises speed and lower risk but brings interface, documentation, obsolescence (DMSMS), and life-cycle-cost concerns (Table 4.2). Software-intensive systems are those where software has "essential influences"; trade-offs between software and physical elements are central.

Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) integrate physical and cyber processes via sensors and feedback (Industry 4.0); networked CPS connected by the Internet form an IoT, which is always an SoS. A System of Systems (SoS) provides a capability none of its constituents can alone; Maier's distinguishing traits are operational and managerial independence (plus geographic distribution, emergent behaviour, evolutionary development). SoS come in four types — directed, acknowledged, collaborative, virtual — and pose challenges of authority, leadership, constituent independence, emergence, and testing. IoT/big-data-driven systems push SE to be more data-driven. Service systems centre on value cocreation through loosely-coupled entities and interface agreements. Enterprise systems apply SE to the enterprise itself — organizations manage resources to create value, individual competence builds organizational/system/operational capability, and enterprise state changes through work processes.

Application by product sector (§4.4)

SE is a generic discipline, but its application varies by domain. Presented alphabetically: Automotive (standards-driven, high-volume, complexity from electrification/autonomy; Mobility-as-a-Service); Biomedical & Healthcare (patient-safety risk over project risk; lean SE for care delivery; device standards like ISO 13485/14971); Commercial Aerospace (complex, increasingly software-rich, part of larger SoS; ARP 4754A); Defense (the birthplace of modern SE; SoS and interoperability focus); Infrastructure (large unique on-site builds, multi-decade life, Table 4.7 maps SE terms to infrastructure terms); Oil & Gas (newer adoption, requirements/digitalization focus, energy transition); Power & Energy (SoS-level, sustainability transition, still immature); Space (high reliability, low maintenance, civil/commercial/national-security split); Telecommunication (enablers for other services, COTS-heavy, critical infrastructure); and Transportation (brownfield in-service SoS, ITS/smart-city complexity).
