Introduction

Chapter 1 lays the conceptual foundation for the entire handbook. Before any talk of stages, models, or processes, it answers four prior questions: what is systems engineering, why does it matter, what concepts must a practitioner master, and what intellectual foundations does the discipline rest on? This review follows the chapter's own order — 1.1 What is SE, 1.2 Why SE matters, 1.3 Systems concepts, 1.4 SE foundations, and 1.5 System science and systems thinking — and reproduces each of the chapter's eight figures with its attribution. Throughout, one theme recurs and is worth carrying forward: every idea here ultimately serves the management of risk and the delivery of systems that are fit for purpose.

What is systems engineering?

The handbook opens from a premise rather than a definition: our world and the systems we engineer keep becoming more complex and interrelated, and systems engineering is the integrative approach that helps teams collaborate to understand and manage that complexity and deliver successful systems. The SE perspective is grounded in systems thinking — an awareness of wholes and how their parts interrelate — and its aim is to ensure the pieces work together to achieve the objectives of the whole. Practitioners work within a project team and take a holistic, balanced, life-cycle approach, carrying responsibility to realise systems that are fit for purpose: systems that accomplish their intended purpose, stay resilient in real-world operation, and minimise unintended actions, side effects, and consequences.

The formal definition, shared by INCOSE Definitions (2019) and ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (2023), is the anchor for everything that follows:

Definition — Systems Engineering
Systems engineering is a transdisciplinary and integrative approach to enable the successful realization, use, and retirement of engineered systems, using systems principles and concepts, and scientific, technological, and management methods.

INCOSE elaborates what SE actually focuses on: establishing, balancing, and integrating stakeholders' goals, success criteria, needs, operational concepts, and required functionality, starting early in the development cycle; establishing an appropriate life-cycle model, process approach, and governance structures given the levels of complexity, uncertainty, change, and variety; generating and evaluating alternative solution concepts and architectures; baselining and modelling requirements and the selected architecture for each stage; performing design synthesis and system verification and validation; and doing all this while considering both the problem and solution domains, accounting for the necessary enabling systems and services, and determining how to balance every factor to reach a satisfactory outcome. SE thereby provides the facilitation, guidance, and leadership that integrate the relevant disciplines and specialty groups into one cohesive effort, proceeding from concept through development, production, utilization, support, and eventual retirement, and weighing both the business and the technical needs of acquirers.

The measure of SE's value
The goal of all SE activities is to manage risk — the risk of not delivering what the acquirer wants and needs, of late delivery, of excess cost, and of negative unintended consequences. One measure of the utility of an SE activity is the degree to which it reduces that risk; conversely, the cost of omitting one is the excess risk incurred as a result.

What a system is

The concept of a system traces back to early philosophy and science, but the version most familiar to SE comes from Ludwig von Bertalanffy (1950, 1968), who regarded a system as a "whole" of interacting "parts." The handbook's definition is deliberately minimal:

Definition — System
A system is an arrangement of parts or elements that together exhibit behaviour or meaning that the individual constituents do not.

A system is sometimes treated as a product, sometimes as the services it provides, and in practice its meaning is often clarified by an associative noun (medical system, aircraft system). A complete system, however, includes all the associated equipment, facilities, material, software, firmware, documentation, services, and personnel required for self-sufficient use in its intended environment. Systems may be physical (matter and energy, exhibiting observable behaviour), conceptual (abstract systems of pure information, exhibiting meaning), or both; in either case their whole-system properties emerge from the elements and their individual properties together with the relationships and interactions among the parts, the whole, external systems, and the environment. SE practitioners are especially concerned with systems that have been or will be engineered for a purpose, so the handbook adds a third definition: an engineered system is a system designed or adapted to interact with an anticipated operational environment to achieve one or more intended purposes while complying with applicable constraints, and may be composed of people, products, services, information, processes, and/or natural elements.

Origins and evolution of SE

Aspects of SE have featured in technical endeavours throughout history, but the discipline was formalised only in the early-to-mid twentieth century. The term "systems engineering" dates to Bell Telephone Laboratories in the early 1940s, with antecedents Fagen (1978) traces to the early 1900s and significant wartime applications; the British analysed their air-defence system with multidisciplinary teams in the 1930s; RAND (founded 1946) claims the creation of "systems analysis"; the first teaching of SE is credited to MIT in 1950 and the first SE book to Goode and Machol in 1957. The professional body was founded as NCOSE in 1990 and renamed INCOSE in 1995 as its membership spread beyond the United States. The international standard ISO/IEC 15288 (2002) formally recognised SE as the preferred mechanism for agreements between acquirers and suppliers, and this handbook builds on the latest, 2023, edition. Table 1.1 in the handbook catalogues the wider family of standards and guides — among them the 24748 life-cycle series, 29148 for requirements, the 42010/42020/42030 architecture standards, 15939 for measurement, 16085 for risk, the 21839–21841 system-of-systems set, the SEBoK, OMG SysML, and the CMMI.

Why is systems engineering important?

The purpose of SE is to conceive, develop, produce, utilise, support, and retire the right product or service within budget and schedule. Delivering the right thing requires a common understanding of the current system state, a shared vision of its future states, and a method to transform stakeholder needs, expectations, and constraints into a solution — a common vision held by acquirers and suppliers alike and achieved through proven, standardised methods applied continuously across the life cycle. SE is particularly important under complexity: most systems today are formed by integrating commercial off-the-shelf products or independently managed systems to obtain emergent capabilities, which raises complexity even as it shortens the path from concept to market. Between 1880 and 2000, the time to reach 25% market penetration fell by more than a factor of four, as Figure 1.1 illustrates; in response, SE has adopted more adaptable and efficient methods — agile approaches, modelling languages such as SysML, and growing Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) — pointing toward a digital-engineering future, with AI and machine learning adding new challenges of verifiability, safety, and trust.

The return on investment of SE

The value of SE has been studied repeatedly. A 2013 study (Honour) quantified the return on investment of SE activity against overall project cost and schedule. As Figure 1.2 shows, increasing the share of SE within a project improves cost compliance (left) and schedule performance (right) up to an optimum, beyond which returns diminish as unwarranted process is added; the data showed SE effort had a significant, quantifiable effect on success, with correlation factors as high as 80% and an optimum SE level of roughly 10–14% of total project cost.

The ROI of adding SE depends on how much is already present, as the handbook's Table 1.2 makes concrete: a project doing no SE can see about a 7:1 return on its first SE investment, falling to roughly 3.5:1 at the median, and turning negative once SE is overdone.

A joint 2012 study by the NDIA, IEEE, and the SEI (Elm and Goldenson) surveyed 148 development projects, sorted them by the maturity of their SE processes, and assessed performance against budget, schedule, and technical requirements. Figure 1.3 shows the result: among projects with lower SE capability, only 15% delivered higher performance and 52% delivered the lowest; at moderate capability the higher-performance share rose to 24% and the lowest fell to 29%; and among projects with higher SE capability, fully 57% delivered higher performance while only 20% delivered the lowest. Well-applied SE plainly increases the probability of successfully developing an engineered system.

Cost committed early, fixed cheaply early

The other half of the economic argument is about timing. A 1993 Defense Acquisition University analysis of US DoD projects examined spent versus committed life-cycle cost over time. As Figure 1.4 shows notionally, by the time roughly 20% of the actual cost has been accrued, well over 80% of the total life-cycle cost has already been committed by the decisions taken — and the figure also shows that the cost to extract a defect climbs steeply the later it is found. Good SE practice is the means by which issues are identified early and that understanding is carried forward, reducing technical debt across the life cycle. INCOSE captures these benefits in value-proposition statements tailored to commercial/market-driven industry, government/infrastructure/aerospace/defence, and non-profit/research enterprises — each turning on SE making development more efficient and competitive and strengthening the capacity to innovate.

Systems concepts

The chapter's longest section equips the practitioner with a working vocabulary. Important concepts include the system of interest, the system environment, and external systems, together with the boundaries between them; the system's modes and states; and the structural ideas of hierarchy and complexity. Each concept corresponds to a class of failure that occurs when it is overlooked.

System boundary and the system of interest

An external view of a system must introduce elements that do not belong to the system but interact with it — the collection of which is the system environment or context, including the users, and not limited to the operating environment but spanning every external system that interfaces with or supports the system at any time in the life cycle. The internal and external views give rise to the system boundary: a line of demarcation between the system under consideration — the system of interest (SoI) — and its greater context, defining what belongs to the system and what does not. A system's functionality is expressed through its interactions with its environment, especially its users; but functionality derives not only from how individual elements interact with the environment but from how those interactions are influenced by the internal organisation of the elements. That observation leads to system architecture, defined by ISO/IEC/IEEE 42020 as the fundamental concepts and properties of an entity in its environment, and the governing principles for its realisation and evolution — a definition spanning both the external and internal views.

Scientific terminology: attributes, states, and the black box

SE draws heavily on the language of science. An attribute is an observable characteristic of a system or element (an aircraft's air speed, for instance); a variable is a symbol naming an attribute; and a measurement assigns a value to a variable through a defined interaction with an observation system. A system is in a state when the values of its attributes hold steady for a meaningful period, and a complete description of state may require values for both attributes and processes (which can be idle or executing). The time evolution of the state is the system's dynamic behaviour, and emergent behaviour is behaviour that cannot be understood from the individual elements alone. The central problem-solving device is the black-box / white-box (opaque-box / transparent-box) pair: the black-box view is the external view (attributes only), the white-box view is the internal view (attributes plus the structure of the elements). Both are useful, and the practitioner must understand how the two relate.

Emergence

Emergence describes the phenomenon that whole entities exhibit properties meaningful only when attributed to the whole, not to its elements — a fundamental property of all systems. Element interactions can create desirable or undesirable emergent properties (reinforcement, or inhibition, interference, and resonance), and emergence can also arise from interaction between the system and its environment; safety and resilience are well-known emergent properties of engineered systems. Architecture and design therefore analyse element interactions deliberately, to reinforce desirable emergence and prevent undesirable emergence, adding derived functions and derived requirements to the baseline where they affect the SoI. Figure 1.5 captures the basic rules: an individual element cannot exhibit system-level emergence; two or more elements are required; and emergence occurs at a level above the individual elements.

Interfacing, interoperating, and enabling systems

External systems lie beyond the SoI boundary and relate to it in three distinct ways. Interfacing systems share an interface — physical, material, energy, or data/information — with the SoI, and humans typically interface with it across its life-cycle stages. Interoperating systems are interfacing systems that interface with the SoI in its operational environment to perform a common function supporting the SoI's primary purpose; the SoI together with its interoperating systems can be seen as a system-of-systems. Enabling systems facilitate the SoI's life-cycle activities without being part of its operational environment, providing services needed during one or more stages — examples include collaboration/development systems, production systems, and logistics support systems. All three must be considered concurrently with the SoI, or important requirements go unidentified and become expensive later; a classic pitfall is to assume a new enabling system will arrive in time, or that an existing one will remain available, when a delay or loss can stall development and deployment. System innovation ecosystem

Where the boundary and external-system ideas describe a system in space, the system innovation ecosystem describes engineered-system innovation as a form of group learning over single and eventually multiple life cycles — by an ecosystem of individuals, teams, enterprises, supply chains, markets, and societies. The descriptive System Innovation Ecosystem Pattern (Schindel and Dove, 2016; Schindel, 2022b) shown in Figure 1.6 identifies three nested top-level boundaries. System 1 — the engineered system is the product, defence system, or service subject to SE life-cycle management (medical devices, aircraft, gas-turbine engines), the engineered SoI of this handbook. System 2 — the life-cycle project-management system provides System 1's environment over its life cycle: a socio-technical system of people, processes, and facilities responsible for learning about System 1 and applying that learning (requirements definition, verification, manufacturing, distribution, sustainment, PLM and digital-twin systems). System 3 — the enterprise process and innovation system contains System 2 and is responsible for learning about and improving it — the organisational change management that advances and adapts how the enterprise engineers. The pattern is deliberately descriptive, not prescriptive: it can characterise any engineering environment, effective in its learning or not, and is meant for planning and analysing real ecosystems.

The hierarchy within a system

A system element is a member of the set of elements that constitute a system — a discrete part that can be implemented to fulfil specified requirements, drawn from hardware, software, data, humans, processes, procedures, facilities, materials, or natural entities. Elements can be atomic (not decomposed further) or systems in their own right (decomposed into subordinate elements); an element can be treated as atomic when a black-box view alone captures its requirements and lets a make, buy, or reuse decision be taken with confidence. Organising elements into distinct subsets subordinated to higher-level systems yields the hierarchy within a system, captured as a Product Breakdown Structure (PBS), as Figure 1.7 illustrates. Urwick's heuristic for span of control — echoing Miller's "seven plus or minus two" — recommends limiting decomposition to about five to nine subordinate elements per level: too few and the level lacks a distinct design activity (design and verification become redundant), too many and the interfaces between subordinates become hard to manage. The relationships across a level form the horizontal view of the system (including requirements and verification artefacts); how those elements derive from higher levels and lead to lower ones forms the vertical view.

States and modes

States and modes are related concepts for defining and modelling functional architectures and managing behaviour. A state is an observable, measurable attribute characterising the system's current configuration, status, or performance-based condition — a snapshot of the variables needed to describe its capability to perform its functions; the list of state variables is fixed, but their values change over time. The current state plus subsequent inputs is enough to compute future states without reference to the full input history, so a state is "the most concise description of the system's past." States often reflect operating conditions (operational, failed, degraded, absent), the physical condition of an element (tank full or empty, battery charged or discharged), or environmental constraints. A mode is a distinct operating capability during which some or all functions may be performed to a full or limited degree — for a computer, "off," "on," "waking up," "reading from disk," "computing," "down." Modes belong to the functional architecture and can be derived by affinity analysis of use cases, organised around mission phases, operating conditions, or special conditions such as test, training, and maintenance; transitions between modes follow decisions, usually triggered when state variables meet defined entry and exit criteria.

Complexity

The chapter closes its concepts section by distinguishing simple, complicated, and complex systems. In a simple system the relationships among element states, once observed, are readily comprehended; in a complicated system they can be unfolded and comprehended, giving sufficient certainty between cause and effect; in a complex system they are so interwoven that they are not fully comprehended, leaving insufficient certainty between cause and effect and producing novel, nonlinear, sometimes counter-intuitive dynamics. Traditional SE takes a reductionist approach — decompose, solve, reassemble — which works well for complicated problems with deterministic, predictable behaviour but performs poorly in complex environments such as autonomous vehicles and other socio-technical systems, where a fundamentally different, iterative and adaptive approach is needed. SE for complex systems therefore balances linear, procedural methods (being systematic) with holistic, nonlinear, iterative methods (being systemic). INCOSE's Complexity Primer identifies fourteen distinguishing characteristics of complexity and stresses that systems are rarely wholly complex — they are typically complex in some characteristics and complicated or simple in others, so SE approaches must be tailored to the actual character of the SoI.

Systems engineering foundations

Beneath the processes lie four foundations that shape how a practitioner thinks and acts: uncertainty, cognitive bias, principles, and heuristics.

Uncertainty

Much SE information and measurement data is uncertain, and the handbook distinguishes two types. Epistemic uncertainty arises from a lack of knowledge — about demand for a new system, about how a technology or process will perform, about an acquirer's preferences — and can be reduced by learning more, which the requirements process and development decisions aim to do. Aleatory uncertainty arises from inherent randomness, present in every performance measurement, and can never be completely removed. The handbook's Table 1.4 catalogues sources of uncertainty across business, political, economic, technological, environmental, and legal dimensions, and notes that uncertainty touches every SE decision process: taking decisions before enough is known is itself a risk, so recognising and managing uncertainty is intrinsic to good SE and feeds directly into risk management.

Cognitive bias

Because practitioners and stakeholders must interpret uncertain information, they are subject to cognitive biases — consistent, predictable errors of judgment caused by simplified information-processing strategies (Tversky and Kahneman, 1974). Biases can affect individuals and teams alike and can contribute to incidents, failures, and disasters through distorted decision-making. The handbook's Table 1.5 lists common biases with mitigations, a selection of which appears below.

For major decisions, more formal external-group methods are most effective — for example NASA's financially and organisationally independent technical authority, or the aviation industry's Crew Resource Management, in which every crew member is responsible for warning of imminent danger.

Systems engineering principles

SE is a relatively young discipline, and a set of principles has emerged over the past thirty years. An SE principle is transcendent — it spans life-cycle stages, system types, and contexts; informs a worldview rather than offering a "how-to"; and is supported by literature or proven practice. Principles are one kind of guidance proposition, alongside heuristics (from experience), conventions (from social agreement), values (from culture), and models (from theory); a proposition becomes a principle when we gain insight into why it works. INCOSE compiled an early list of 8 principles and 61 sub-principles in 1993, which fed the SE processes; the current set (INCOSE SE Principles, 2022) comprises 15 principles and 20 sub-principles. Among the most orienting are: SE in application is specific to stakeholder needs and context throughout the life cycle; SE keeps a holistic system view of elements, interactions, enabling systems, and environment; SE decisions are made under uncertainty, accounting for risk; SE spans the entire system life cycle; complex systems are engineered by complex organisations; SE integrates engineering and scientific disciplines; and SE is based on a middle-range set of theories with systems, physical/logical, mathematical, and sociological bases. Applying the principles helps determine a life-cycle model, implement the processes, and define the organisational constructs around the work.

Systems engineering heuristics

Where principles explain why, heuristics pass on a profession's accumulated wisdom as short, memorable, natural-language "rules of thumb." They are most valuable for inherently complex, unbounded, or ill-structured problems where analytical methods are limited by the sheer number of variables and stakeholder interactions. The tradition is ancient: cathedral builders encoded their craft in rules like "maintain a low centre of gravity" and "put 80% of the mass in the pillars," with conservative margins and lessons learned from each collapse, long before the underlying science existed. Renewed interest stems from Maier and Rechtin's work on the system architect, who often found it better to apply heuristics than to attempt detailed analysis. Representative SE heuristics include: don't assume the original statement of the problem is necessarily the best or even the right one — failure to reach mutual understanding with stakeholders early is a fundamental cause of failure; in the early stages, unknowns are a bigger issue than known problems; and model before you build, wherever possible, since "the only complete model of the system in its environment is the system in its environment." A good heuristics repository links to other knowledge sources, is tagged for flexible retrieval, and lets practitioners assemble the personal set most relevant to their work.

System science and systems thinking

The chapter ends by situating SE within its intellectual foundations and describing the virtuous cycle that links them. Interrelating concepts such as boundary, relationship, environment, hierarchy, emergence, communication, and control with purpose produces a systems worldview, and reasoning from it is systems thinking, which lets us recognise patterns across different phenomena and disciplines. Studying those patterns produced the systems sciences — General System Theory, Cybernetics, and Complexity Theory — and their methodologies and methods; applying these in engineering, then reflecting on the results, improves both systems science and systems thinking and enhances our ability to design and intervene in complex systems. Figure 1.8 depicts this as a purposeful activity to deliver elegant solutions to complex problems, supported by principles that guide why, what, and how we do SE: motivational principles express our intentions; transdisciplinary principles organise our technique principles; and the systemic relationships among the principles support how they guide the relationships between SE's purpose, approach, and practice.

Systems science

Questions about systems, organisation, and complexity are not new, but systems science can be defined as a transdisciplinary approach with two goals: to identify and understand patterns of behaviour that cross disciplinary fields, and to establish a general theory applicable to all kinds of systems — physical, natural, engineered, or social. It takes both reductionist and holistic forms, and both are valuable: a clock's workings can be explained reductively (and agent-based modelling, pioneered at the Santa Fe Institute, builds whole-system behaviour from the bottom up), while a holistic approach explains why we need clocks and how they operate, sustain, degrade, and evolve over a life cycle. Reductionism alone cannot account for emergent properties, which is why Ludwig von Bertalanffy promoted a General System Theory of laws applying to systems in general — motivated by the many parallels (growth, hierarchical order, equifinality, open and closed systems, goal-directedness) that recur across systems regardless of their elements. Together with Cybernetics and Complexity Theory, the systems sciences seek a common language and foundations that make rigorous systems theory accessible to practitioners and able to serve a transdisciplinary meta-discipline like SE.

Systems thinking

Systems thinking is a key enabler of SE and one of the core competencies in the INCOSE SE Competency Framework. It applies the properties, concepts, and principles of systems to a situation as a framework for curiosity — to gain insight and understanding — and demands a balance between being systematic (applying the SE processes) and being systemic (applying systems thinking to drive them). Though it lacks a single agreed definition, one widely cited formulation is DSRP: we make Distinctions between things and ideas, identify Systems of parts and wholes, recognise Relationships of action and reaction, and take Perspectives made of a point and a view (Cabrera et al., 2015). Building on the pioneering work of von Bertalanffy, Wiener, Forrester, and Checkland, many systems methodologies, models, and methods have been formalised; the practitioner who knows what each does well can choose among them. Jackson and Keys's System of Systems Methodologies (SOSM), later updated to incorporate the Cynefin framework, classifies methodologies by their strengths in handling complexity and reconciling divergent viewpoints. Using different approaches in informed combination, tailored to the technical, organisational, cultural, and political factors of a situation, is called Critical Systems Thinking — a multi-perspectival, multi-methodological, multi-method approach. Most of these methods grew up in the management sciences, but they transfer directly to SE, whose problems demand exactly the same cultural, social, political, and managerial perspectives.

Key takeaways

Chapter 1 establishes the conceptual ground for everything that follows. Systems engineering is an integrative, transdisciplinary, life-cycle discipline whose deepest purpose is to manage risk and deliver systems that are fit for purpose. A system is an arrangement of parts whose value lies in the behaviour or meaning the whole exhibits that the parts do not — which makes emergence, the system boundary, and the surrounding interfacing, interoperating, and enabling systems concepts to reason about together. SE matters most under complexity, and its value is measurable: there is an optimum level of SE investment, project performance rises with SE capability, and most life-cycle cost is committed early — so the cheapest time to get things right is at the start. Beneath the processes lie the foundations of uncertainty, cognitive bias, principles, and heuristics; and beneath those lie systems science and systems thinking, joined to practice in a virtuous cycle. Carry these concepts and this vocabulary forward, and the life-cycle stages, models, and processes of the chapters ahead will read as a natural elaboration of them.
