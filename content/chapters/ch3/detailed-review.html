3.0 Overview — life cycle analyses and methods

Part II of the handbook laid out the SE life-cycle processes. Chapter 3 turns to the topics, techniques, and methods that cut across those processes, reflecting the concurrent, iterative, and recursive nature of SE. It divides into two sections: §3.1 Quality Characteristics and approaches (the specialty-engineering "‑ilities") and §3.2 Systems Engineering analyses and methods.

3.1.1 Introduction to quality characteristics

ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (2023, §3.36) defines a quality characteristic (QC) as an "inherent characteristic of a product, process, or system related to a requirement." QCs are how stakeholders judge quality. The approaches that ensure QCs are present were previously known as specialty engineering and are also called Design for X (DFX) and through-life considerations; informally they are the ‑ilities. QC approaches are life-cycle perspectives that ensure the QCs are present when the system is produced, utilized, supported, and retired — and they require looking beyond the engineered system to the SoS or enterprise it belongs to and its interoperating and enabling systems. They mostly generate non-functional requirements, but safety, security, and resilience may also generate functional requirements. The handbook presents them alphabetically on purpose, to avoid weighting one over another.

3.1.2 Affordability analysis

Definition: an approach that maximizes value, providing cost-effective capability over the entire life cycle. INCOSE defines system affordability as "the balance of system performance, cost, and schedule constraints over the system life while satisfying mission needs in concert with strategic investment and organizational needs." A system is affordable if it can be developed to meet requirements within cost and schedule constraints — but quantifying affordability is hard and contextually sensitive. Three key concepts: define the affordability context/system/portfolio consistently; establish and document an affordability process/framework; and assign accountability (governance) across the life cycle.

Because two systems with similar output capabilities are differentiated by their non-functional attributes, the affordability model concerns the operational attributes — the quality characteristics — that determine value over time.

Managing within an affordability trade space means relating actual performance to cost over time. Quantifying the cost–performance–time relationship lets engineers find the point of diminishing returns. In Figure 3.3, fixing capabilities and schedule leaves either cost or performance as the decision criterion while the other becomes the constraint; the shaded region holds the affordable, compliant solutions, and the curve holds the "best value" solutions.

Cost-effectiveness (CE) = system effectiveness ÷ (initial cost + sustainment cost); reliability and maintainability heavily influence sustainment cost. CE is distinct from cost–benefit analysis (CBA), which expresses outcomes in monetary terms (enabling comparison of projects with different goals). Life Cycle Cost (LCC) is the total cost across the system's life and should not be equated with Total Cost of Ownership (TCO), Total Ownership Cost (TOC), or Whole Life Cost (WLC), which often count only post-acquisition costs. LCC estimates are imprecise early (based on assumptions) and improve as actual data accrues; their main purpose is to identify cost drivers.

LCC estimating methods (mixable): analogy, bottom-up, Delphi, design-to-cost (DTC), expert judgment, parametric/algorithmic (e.g., COSYSMO for SE effort, COCOMO for software), Parkinsonian, price-to-win, taxonomy, and top-down.

3.1.3 Agility engineering

Definition: an approach that enables change in a timely and cost-effective manner. Agility is the ability to thrive in uncertain, unpredictable environments; effective response has four metrics — timely, affordable, predictable, comprehensive. Distinguish agile systems engineering (engineering process) from engineering an agile system (the product), which is this section's subject. Sustained agility comes from a "drag-and-drop, plug-and-play" loosely-coupled modular architecture (think Lego). Three architectural elements: encapsulated modules, a passive infrastructure (just-enough rules/standards balancing requisite variety and parsimony), and an active infrastructure (five designated responsibilities: module-mix evolution, module readiness, situational awareness, system assembly, infrastructure evolution). Ten reusable/reconfigurable/scalable design principles support it, and agility is measured by time, cost, predictability, and scope of response.

3.1.4 Human systems integration (HSI)

Definition: an approach that integrates technology, organizations, and people effectively. HSI is an essential, transdisciplinary, sociotechnical approach ensuring the technical, organizational, and human elements are addressed across the whole life cycle. It adopts a sociotechnical view in which every system contains both humans and machines, and considers integration from two angles: integrating human and technological elements, and integrating the different human/machine perspectives.

HSI rests on four communities of practice — human factors/ergonomics, information technology, systems engineering, and the operational domain — and is delivered chiefly through Human-Centered Design (HCD) with human-in-the-loop (HITL) activities. Its purpose is to optimize total system performance and stakeholder satisfaction through the mutual integration of technology, organizations, people, and environment, with benefits spanning safety, cost, usability, UX, and adoption.

3.1.5 Interoperability analysis

Definition: an approach that ensures the system interacts effectively with other systems. The handbook cites four standard definitions, all stressing exchanging and using information. Interoperability is achieved two ways, which may be combined: adopting one or more published standards (consumer "plug and play") and/or defining a custom interface where no suitable standard exists. It is a key enabler of a System of Systems and grows in importance with the IoT and coalition operations. (The Øresund Bridge shows the interoperability challenges of just two nations — meshing health-and-safety regulations, left- vs. right-handed railways, and two railway power systems.)

3.1.6 Logistics engineering

Definition: an approach that enables support for the entire life cycle. Also called product-support engineering, it identifies, acquires, and provisions the support resources to sustain operation and maintenance, and engineers the inherent supportability of the design. Its scope: determine support requirements, design for supportability, acquire/procure support, and provide cost-effective support during utilization. It evolved into supply-chain management (commercial) and integrated logistics support / ILS (defence). The classic support elements include product-support management, design interface, maintenance planning, operation/maintenance personnel, training, supply support, computer resources, technical data, facilities/infrastructure, PHS&T (packaging, handling, storage, transportation), and support equipment. Supportability analysis uses functional/physical failure analysis (FMECA, FTA, RBD), task identification (corrective via FMECA, preventive via RCM), LORA (level-of-repair analysis), modelling/simulation (including digital twins), and recording & corrective action.

3.1.7 Manufacturability / producibility analysis

Definition: an approach that enables production in a responsible and cost-effective manner. A system that cannot be effectively produced causes rework, delays, and cost overruns. Producibility considerations differ with production volume (satellites = limited runs; tanks = medium; phones = high). A key decision is whether existing production enabling systems are satisfactory (lowest risk/cost) or must be developed in time — and the enabling system can cost more than the system being produced. Multidisciplinary teams simplify the design and stabilize processes to reduce risk, cost, lead time, and material use; long-lead, sole-source, and special-process items are evaluated early; design simplification also eases assembly/disassembly and recycling. (IKEA is the exemplar of producibility-and-transportability-driven design.)

3.1.8 Reliability, availability, maintainability (RAM)

Definition: an approach that enables the system to perform without failure, to be operational when needed, and to be restored to a required functional state. RAM (or RMA) QCs are interrelated and tied to logistics/supportability. Treat them as non-functional requirements that often drive other requirements — plan them early and integrate with the SEMP. In many industries, availability is seen as the most important to a user/acquirer, because availability loss translates directly to mission/production loss.

Reliability

Reliability is "the ability of a system to perform as designed, without failure, in an operational environment, for a stated period of time." Designing reliable systems requires understanding the interaction between the system, its environment, its operating conditions, and potential failure modes and mechanisms.

Reliability engineering has four objectives in priority order: (1) prevent/reduce the likelihood of failures; (2) identify and correct causes of failures that occur; (3) cope with failures whose causes are not corrected; (4) estimate reliability of new designs and analyze data. The modern shift is from reactive "test-analyze-fix" to proactive design-for-reliability and failure-mode avoidance. A common trap: MTBF is not "expected life" — prefer reliability (success probability) at a specific time, or failure-free period. Activities split into engineering analyses/tests (FEA, CFD, ALT/HALT, stress analysis) and failure analyses (FMEA, FTA, RBD, Monte Carlo, root-cause, reliability growth). ANSI/GEIA-STD-0009 is the supporting reliability program standard.

Availability

Availability is the ratio of uptime to total time, so it depends on reliability (uptime) and maintainability + logistics (downtime). It is formally "the probability that a system, when used under stated conditions, will operate satisfactorily at any point in time as required," and comes in three forms:

  • Inherent availability (Ai) — based only on inherent reliability and maintainability; assumes an ideal support environment; excludes preventive maintenance, logistics delay, and administrative delay.
  • Achieved availability (Aa) — like Ai but includes preventive (scheduled) maintenance; still excludes logistics and administrative delay.
  • Operational availability (Ao) — assumes the actual operational environment and therefore includes logistics delay and administrative delay.
Exam focus — availability
The three availability types are a perennial exam item. Track what each adds: Ai = design (reliability + maintainability) only → Aa = + preventive maintenance → Ao = + logistics & administrative delays. A Service Level Agreement (SLA) typically specifies availability over a period.

Maintainability

Maintainability is the built-in ability of a system to be repaired and restored — it must be designed in, whereas maintenance is the resulting action. Maintenance breaks down into corrective (unscheduled, after failure), preventive (scheduled, to retain performance), predictive (condition-based), and system upgrades. The most-used metric is MTTR (mean time to repair) — covering fault detection/isolation, disassembly, repair, reassembly, and test — typically lognormally distributed. Testability (BIT, diagnostic equipment) and interchangeability/accessibility are key maintainability design concerns. Reliability engineering aims to prevent failure; safety engineering aims to prevent/mitigate harm; logistics aims at efficient support — all share "failure" themes and benefit from early implementation, and an FMEA serves reliability, safety, and logistics with different objectives.

3.1.9 Resilience engineering

Definition: an approach that provides required capability when facing adversity. A relatively new term (popularized ~2010), resilience typically subsumes survivability. SE resilience identifies the required capabilities, the adverse conditions under which they must be delivered, and the architecture/design that ensures delivery. Crucially, resilience is about preserving capability, not the architecture — continuity and adaptability are both valid. Adversity is anything that might degrade capability, from any source (environmental, human, system failure) and type (adversarial/friendly/neutral, malicious/accidental, expected/not), arising inside or outside the system.

Resilience uses a two-layer taxonomy. Layer 1 — fundamental objectives: avoid (reduce exposure), withstand (resist degradation), recover (replenish lost capability). Layer 2 — means objectives, including adaptive response, agility, anticipation, constrain, continuity, disaggregation, evolution, graceful degradation, integrity, prepare, prevent, re-architect, redeploy, robustness, situational awareness, tolerance, transform, and understand. Resilience must be considered early, especially when developing requirements, and managed in the trade space thereafter.

Resilience requirements often take the form of a resilience scenario (system/mode/state encountering an adversity that imposes a stress, affecting delivery of a capability over a timeframe, subject to constraints, to achieve a resilience target). System-state transitions group into three categories: robustness (maintain capability), tolerance (degrade to a lower, still-useful level), and recovery (return to a higher level). Resilience shares much with availability, maintainability, reliability, safety, security, and sustainability — together the loss-driven areas (see §3.1.13).

3.1.10 Sustainability engineering

Definition: an approach that supports the circular economy over its life. Design for sustainability considers environmental and social aspects as key design elements to reduce harmful impacts across the life cycle, promoting responsible disposability via recycling and materials reuse. The circular economy shares, reuses, repairs, and recycles to extend product life, minimize waste, and create a closed loop — consistent with the UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals. Environmental impact analysis spans the full life cycle; disposal analysis and design for disassembly maximize residual value. Tools include the ISO 14000 series, Environmental Product Declarations (EPD), and life-cycle assessment/management (LCA, LCIA, LCO, LCM). A circular system relies on reliability, maintainability, logistics, resilience, and manufacturability.

3.1.11 System safety engineering

Definition: an approach that reduces the likelihood of harm to people, assets, and the wider environment. The goal is to reduce and mitigate hazards to an acceptable level of risk — engineered systems are never 100% safe. Safety extends beyond the SoI to everyone involved in production, utilization, support, and retirement, and to third parties.

Safety is an emergent property of the system in its real operational environment — it is not the sum of element-level safety/reliability, and how the system is used, maintained, and managed matters as much as its inherent design. "Acceptably safe" varies by industry and country; typical bars are: regulatory compliance (simple standardized elements), ALARP — "as low as reasonably practicable" (complicated safety-critical civilian systems), or ALARP-or-better-than-the-alternative (military, medical, emergency systems). Regulators assess both the likelihood a hazard leads to harm and the severity of harm.

The control hierarchy, most-to-least preferred: eliminate the hazard by design > reduce likelihood/severity with design mitigations > operational/maintenance controls, warnings, and training. A near miss is an unmitigated hazardous situation that happened to cause no harm — critical feedback whose handling depends on safety culture. Most accidents are multi-causal; a hazardous situation can occur without any element failure (design/implementation error, or misaligned mental models of designers, operators, and maintainers). Hazard sources are intrinsic, functional, socio-technical, and management/cultural. A tailored safety management system, suitably qualified people, and a strong safety culture ("how we behave when no one is looking") sustain safety through life; agile methods warrant caution for safety-critical systems.

3.1.12 System security engineering (SSE)

Definition: an approach that identifies, protects from, detects, responds to, and recovers from anomalous and disruptive events, including in a cyber-contested environment. SSE applies SE principles to analyze threats and vulnerabilities and mitigate risks to system assets across the life cycle, blending technology, management, and operational rules. Threats may be external (theft, denial-of-service, power loss) or internal (user actions, supporting systems), and unintentional (misuse) or intentional (malicious). Physical security guards against physical actions (theft, vandalism, intrusion) with layered controls; cybersecurity protects the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information assets. Trustworthiness spans privacy, reliability, resilience, safety, and security. Per NIST SP 800-160 Vol. 1 & 2 (built on ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 and this handbook), the best way to secure systems is through the SE process — integrating security into each life-cycle process rather than bolting it on. (Case Study 3, the Stuxnet attack, illustrates its importance.)

3.1.13 Loss-driven systems engineering (LDSE)

SE methodologies are largely capability-driven and can give fragmented attention to potential losses. LDSE is the value-adding unification of the loss-driven QCs — resilience, safety, security, sustainability/disposability, and availability — so they collaborate on the adversities considered; the weaknesses, defects, flaws, exposures, hazards, and vulnerabilities considered; the assets and losses considered; and the coping mechanisms. Practitioners should elicit loss-driven requirements as an integrated part of stakeholder/system requirements, make architecture and design decisions holistically across these areas, and fold their risks into one risk-management activity.

3.2.1 Modeling, analysis, and simulation (MA&S)

The INCOSE SE Vision 2035 predicts a model-based future. The essential artifact of MA&S is an explicit model — an idealized representation of one or more aspects of the as-is or to-be SoI. The three activities are distinct: modeling conceives, creates, and refines models; analysis is systematic, reproducible examination to gain insight; simulation uses a model to predict and study behaviour. In this handbook, simulation always involves a digital model, and any examination involving a physical model is a test. Digital models are often parameterized so one model covers many configurations ("experiments," a.k.a. analysis/load/verification cases).

MA&S supports every life-cycle process — refining descriptive models in Business/Mission Analysis and Stakeholder Needs, flowing down System Requirements, synthesizing and comparing alternatives in Architecture/Design, exploring the trade space in System Analysis, supporting Implementation/Integration/Verification (including hardware- and software-in-the-loop), and building digital twins for Operation and Maintenance. It also enables early, iterative V&V across these processes.

Models are classified to aid selection. Physical models use real parts (a physical-model simulation is a test); digital models are formal (machine-readable, defined semantics — logical/descriptive, quantitative/mathematical, geometric, or surrogate) or informal (human convention, not necessarily machine-readable); mixed models combine both. Any model is further characterized by breadth, granularity, and fidelity.

The biggest MA&S advantage is separation of representation and presentation: a model-based "authoritative source of truth" can generate many correct-by-construction views, overcoming the document-centric problem of duplicated, hard-to-maintain information. Other benefits include more explicit SE, better problem specification, rigorous design, early V&V to reduce risk, multi-user collaboration, better change-impact assessment, and mastering complexity. Because SE coordinates all disciplines, SE MA&S must interoperate with each discipline's modelling — done through a hub-and-spokes pattern with an integrated system-model repository at the hub.

Across the life cycle, multiple disciplines share the integrated system-model repository — the "authoritative source of truth," logically one repository even if physically federated, with versioning, branching, and merging. When organizations protect IP, they exchange black-box (opaque) models that hide internals while exposing an interface, versus white-box (transparent) models that reveal them.

3.2.2 Prototyping

Prototyping enhances awareness and understanding of stakeholder needs. A prototype is not the "first draft" of a production entity — it is intended to enhance learning and should be set aside once that purpose is achieved (the production entity may behave differently). Two types: rapid prototyping is an easy, fast way to get performance data and evaluate concepts, quickly assembled from existing physical/graphical/mathematical elements (3-D printing, simulation shells), used for form, fit, human-system interface, operations, or producibility; and traditional prototyping, which reduces risk/uncertainty in two variants — a partial prototype verifies critical elements, and a full prototype is a complete, accurate representation yielding objective performance and error-rate data.

3.2.3 Traceability

Traceability is "the ability to trace the history, application, or location of an object/entity/item" (ISO 9000) — for SE, an association between two or more items (concepts, needs, requirements, architecture, design, verification, validation, models, supplied elements). Three types:

  • Bidirectional traceability — tracing an item to another while automatically establishing the reverse link; facilitated by SE tools with two-way links.
  • Vertical traceability — across organizational/architectural levels (Level n, n+1, …), referred to as parent/child relationships.
  • Horizontal traceability — across elements at the same level (peer relationships) and across life-cycle stages/processes (e.g., stakeholder requirement → system requirements → architecture/design → realized product → verification/validation artifacts).

Traceability is facilitated by the Configuration Management process, which assigns unique identifiers that, once issued, are never reused. It is also crucial to the digital thread: in an MBSE environment the model lets a requirement be traced through functional and physical representations to specific configurations — and the reverse, identifying which requirements are affected when a physical element changes.

3.2.4 Interface management

Interface Management facilitates and manages the identification, definition, design, and management of interfaces across the life cycle — boundaries, the interactions across them, and the interface requirements. It cuts across the Agreement, Technical Management, and Technical processes and should be treated as a distinct activity, because failing to define interactions causes costly rework during integration, verification, and validation. For complex systems, projects develop a separate Interface Management Plan and often use an Interface Control Working Group. Of particular importance is the Human-Machine Interface (HMI). Modern software-intensive systems multiply interactions — a modern car may have 150+ computer modules.

During requirements and architecture the focus is on what is involved in each interaction (its characteristics), not how it is realized (deferred to Design Definition, which fixes the media — data bus, harness, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth). Definitions are recorded in interface control artifacts — an Interface Control Document (ICD), Interface Definition Document (IDD), Interface Agreement Document (IAD), or Data Dictionary — placed under Configuration Management. A data-centric (model/database) practice enables effective impact and change analysis. Interface analysis tools include context/boundary diagrams, FFBDs, DFDs, IPO diagrams, FMEA, STPA, SysML, and the N² diagram.

An N² diagram is an N×N matrix; when all elements are compared to all others, the matrix is complete, and lower-level interactions can expand into lower-level N² diagrams. The Design Structure Matrix (DSM) looks similar but uses the opposite convention (inputs on rows, outputs on columns), so interactions flow counterclockwise. Coupling matrices (a type of N² diagram) define aggregates and integration order, keeping interfaces as simple as possible; reorganizing the matrix groups elements into aggregates and minimizes the interfaces verified between them.

Exam focus — N² vs. DSM
N² diagram: outputs horizontal, inputs vertical, interactions flow clockwise. DSM: the reverse (inputs on rows, outputs on columns → counterclockwise). Both put elements on the diagonal; blank off-diagonal cells mean no interaction. The coupling matrix optimizes aggregate definition for integration.

3.2.5 Architecture frameworks

An architecture description framework is "a set of conventions, principles and practices for the description of architectures established within a specific domain of application or community of stakeholders" (ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2022). (TOGAF defines an AF more broadly as a foundational structure for developing a range of architectures.) Frameworks promote harmonization, consistency, and reuse, especially across distributed teams. Most are organized around one or more viewpoints covering target domains and stakeholder concerns — e.g., NAF, UAF, DoDAF — and may add methods, tools/vocabulary, and multiple dimensions.

Frameworks support the key architecture activities: enablement (PEAF, GERAM), governance (enterprise- and project-level, per NAF/TOGAF), management (an architecting management plan), description (42010 views/viewpoints, 42020 conceptualization/elaboration; Zachman shows how viewpoints characterize the problem and represent the solution), and evaluation (42030; methods such as ATAM and MFESA). Per Sowa and Zachman, two levels of framework — enterprise and product — keep products and systems consistent with enterprise strategy.

3.2.6 Patterns

Patterns are recurrences — repeated regularities observed across time, space, or other dimensions — and lie at the heart of the physical sciences and engineering. In SE, patterns appear in requirements, architectures, value, failure modes, and the socio-technical systems of engineering. They have both fixed (recurring) and variable (parameterized) aspects and may be informal or formal. Informal patterns live in expertise/tribal knowledge or explicit-but-informal prose templates (Alexander's civil-architecture patterns; the Gang-of-Four software design patterns) — practitioners should not underestimate their value for transmitting knowledge.

Formal patterns are model-based, enabling automated configuration and conformance-checking — the natural complement to MBSE. The S*Metamodel is a formal, tool-neutral answer to "what is the smallest model needed to represent a system across its life cycle?" An S*Model is any model mapped to that metamodel. Three example truths from the S*Metamodel: all behaviour is interaction-based; requirement statements are transfer functions (input-output relationships parameterized by state); and stakeholder value, failure effects, and product-line configurability are manifestations of the same variables.

S*Patterns are reusable, configurable S*Models of system families, used across automotive, aerospace, medical-device, and other domains. Pattern-based methods also address "what about what we already know?" — the System Innovation Ecosystem Pattern curates trusted knowledge alongside newly extracted, validated information, managing pattern credibility across programs.

3.2.7 Design thinking

Design Thinking complements systems thinking by exploring human needs; the business/operational reasoning by which design concepts are devised (especially creative ones); the systems being realized; their specialization; and their utility/value for stakeholders. It uses context analysis and problem framing to explore and restructure the problem, and ideation to generate candidate solutions — drawing on known solutions/patterns, design reasoning, iteration between functional requirements and solutions (Axiomatic Design), and successive divergent and convergent phases. It helps challenge assumptions, redefine problems, anticipate emergence, and realize innovative solutions across business/mission analysis, requirements, architecture, and design.

3.2.8 Biomimicry

Natural systems include living and non-living systems — anything not human-made. Biomimicry is "a practice that learns from and mimics the strategies found in nature to solve human design challenges." Nature-inspired SE can improve capability, efficiency, and performance — optimized information processing/sensing, operation in extreme environments, novel materials, distributed architectures, lowered environmental impact, and resilience — by repeatedly asking "Can nature help me solve this problem?"

To develop nature-inspired solutions, the practitioner stays open to alternatives; defines requirements as abstract functions/goals with metrics; searches for natural systems that satisfy the function; selects candidates; abstracts the natural strategy; explores architectural variations; transfers the strategy to the SoI; and evaluates performance and environmental impact. Partnering with natural-systems scientists in a cross-disciplinary team is often essential.
