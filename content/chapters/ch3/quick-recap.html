What this chapter covers

Chapter 3 steps outside the sequential life-cycle processes of Chapter 2 and gathers the cross-cutting analyses and methods that a systems engineer applies concurrently, iteratively, and recursively across the whole life cycle. It has two halves. Section 3.1 — Quality Characteristics (QC) and approaches covers the "‑ilities": the specialty-engineering perspectives (affordability, RAM, safety, security, resilience, and so on) that ensure the system is fit for purpose. Section 3.2 — Systems Engineering analyses and methods covers the techniques that cut across the technical processes (modelling and simulation, prototyping, traceability, interface management, architecture frameworks, patterns, design thinking, and biomimicry).

Exam focus — ASEP
Chapter 3 is heavy on definitions and vocabulary. The ASEP exam rewards knowing the one-line definition of each QC approach, the representative QCs it owns, and the few high-yield distinctions: the three availability types, the resilience triad (avoid / withstand / recover), ALARP, the three traceability types, the N²-diagram clockwise convention, and model vs. analysis vs. simulation. Spend your time on these rather than the long descriptive prose.

Quality characteristics (the "‑ilities")

ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 (2023) defines a quality characteristic (QC) as an "inherent characteristic of a product, process, or system related to a requirement." QCs are how stakeholders judge the quality of the system. The approaches that deliver them were previously called specialty engineering (or the engineering specialties) and are also known as Design for X (DFX) and through-life considerations. They are informally the ‑ilities. QC approaches are life-cycle perspectives that mostly generate non-functional requirements — though safety, security, and resilience may also generate functional requirements. The handbook lists them in alphabetical order on purpose, so as not to imply that any one matters more than another.

A thirteenth idea, Loss-Driven Systems Engineering (LDSE), unifies the QCs that focus on potential losses — resilience, safety, security, sustainability/disposability, and availability — so they share adversities, assets/losses, and coping mechanisms rather than being treated in isolation.

Affordability, cost-effectiveness, and LCC

A system is affordable if it can be developed and operated within cost and schedule constraints while meeting mission needs. Cost-effectiveness relates achieved system effectiveness to cost. Life Cycle Cost (LCC) is the total cost across concept, development, production, utilization & support, and retirement — and should not be equated with Total Cost of Ownership, which often counts only post-acquisition costs. LCC estimating methods include analogy, bottom-up, Delphi, design-to-cost, expert judgment, parametric (e.g., COSYSMO, COCOMO), Parkinsonian, price-to-win, taxonomy, and top-down.

RAM — reliability, availability, maintainability

These three QCs are tightly interrelated and tied to logistics. Reliability is performing without failure for a stated period in a given environment; modern practice favours proactive design-for-reliability over reactive test-analyze-fix, and warns that MTBF is not "expected life." Availability is the ratio of uptime to total time and comes in three flavours: inherent (Ai) — design only, ideal support, no preventive maintenance; achieved (Aa) — adds preventive maintenance; operational (Ao) — adds logistics and administrative delays. Maintainability is the built-in ability to be restored; maintenance is the action. Maintenance types: corrective, preventive, predictive, and upgrades; the key metric is MTTR.

Resilience, safety, and security

Resilience = providing required capability when facing adversity; its three fundamental objectives are avoid, withstand, recover, achieved through means objectives (anticipate, prepare, adapt, graceful degradation, etc.). System-state transitions are categorised as robustness (maintain level), tolerance (degrade gracefully), and recovery (return to higher capability). Safety is an emergent property; no system is 100% safe, so hazards are reduced to "acceptably safe" — often ALARP (as low as reasonably practicable). The control hierarchy is eliminate > reduce likelihood/severity by design > operational/maintenance controls. A near miss is an unmitigated hazardous situation that happened to cause no harm. Security protects confidentiality, integrity, and availability; trustworthiness spans privacy, reliability, resilience, safety, and security, and is anchored in NIST SP 800-160.

HSI, interoperability, logistics, producibility, sustainability, agility

HSI integrates Technology, Organization, and People (the TOP model) within an environment, using Human-Centered Design. Interoperability is achieved via agreed published standards and/or custom interfaces, and is a key enabler of a System of Systems. Logistics engineering engineers supportability and provides cost-effective support; it spans the classic support elements (supply support, support equipment, training, technical data, facilities, PHS&T, etc.). Manufacturability/producibility makes the system economical to build — sometimes the production enabling system costs more than the product. Sustainability supports the circular economy (reuse, repair, recycle, design-for-disassembly) and aligns with the ISO 14000 series. Agility engineers systems for timely, affordable, predictable, comprehensive response via loosely-coupled, plug-and-play modular architectures.

Systems engineering analyses and methods

Section 3.2 covers methods that cut across the technical processes.

  • Modeling, Analysis & Simulation (MA&S) — the essential artifact is an explicit model. Modeling creates models; analysis is systematic reproducible examination; simulation uses a model to predict behaviour (a physical-model examination is a test). Models are physical or digital, formal or informal; SE mostly uses digital, formal models. MBSE makes the model the "authoritative source of truth," integrating disciplines through a hub-and-spokes pattern and enabling early V&V.
  • Prototyping — enhances learning and is set aside afterwards. Rapid (quick, from existing elements; form/fit/interface) vs. traditional (partial = verify critical elements, full = complete representation).
  • Traceability — associating items. Bidirectional (auto reverse link), vertical (parent/child across levels), horizontal (peer across a level / across the life cycle). Facilitated by Configuration Management and the digital thread.
  • Interface Management — identifies, defines, and controls interfaces; recorded in an Interface Control Document (ICD), often run by an Interface Control Working Group. The N² diagram is the classic tool: elements on the diagonal, interactions flow clockwise (outputs horizontal, inputs vertical). A coupling matrix (a type of N² diagram) optimizes aggregate definition for integration.
  • Architecture Frameworks — conventions for describing architectures (ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010 defines views and viewpoints). Examples: TOGAF, DoDAF, NAF, UAF, Zachman; ATAM and MFESA support architecture evaluation.
  • Patterns — recurring regularities, informal (expertise, prose) or formal (model-based, e.g., S*Patterns), reused and configured into specific models.
  • Design Thinking — a human-needs-centred, divergent/convergent complement to systems thinking.
  • Biomimicry — learning from and mimicking nature's strategies to solve design challenges.
