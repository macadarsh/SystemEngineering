Chapter 3 — Core Concepts

A deeper, exam-weighted pass through the quality-characteristic approaches and the cross-cutting SE methods — the definitions, the representative -ilities, and the high-yield distinctions (the three availability types, ALARP, the resilience triad, the traceability types, the N² convention). Builds on the Quick Recap and prepares the figure-by-figure Detailed Review.

Orientation: two kinds of cross-cutting work

Chapter 3 collects the work that does not belong to any single life-cycle process but is applied across all of them. Keep two buckets in mind. Quality Characteristics (QC) approaches (§3.1) are the specialty-engineering "‑ilities" — they decide whether the system is fit for purpose beyond raw function. SE analyses and methods (§3.2) are the reusable techniques — modelling, traceability, interfaces, frameworks, patterns — that make the technical processes tractable. Both are applied concurrently, iteratively, and recursively.

Exam focus — ASEP
For each QC approach, lock in three things: (1) its one-line "an approach that…" definition, (2) the representative QCs it owns, and (3) one or two signature distinctions (e.g., the three availability types, ALARP, avoid/withstand/recover). The exam tests recognition of these far more than the surrounding narrative.

Quality characteristics and approaches (§3.1)

A quality characteristic is an inherent characteristic of a product, process, or system related to a requirement (ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288:2023, §3.36). QC approaches mostly generate non-functional requirements; safety, security, and resilience can also generate functional requirements. The handbook orders them alphabetically deliberately, and notes that not every approach applies to every system — subject-matter experts should be consulted.

Affordability analysis

Affordability is "the balance of system performance, cost, and schedule constraints over the system life while satisfying mission needs." It is contextually sensitive — you must fix the affordability context, the system(s), and the portfolio, establish a documented framework, and assign accountability (governance) across the life cycle. The affordability trade space relates performance, cost, and time; fixing capabilities and schedule leaves cost or performance as the decision criterion while the other becomes a constraint, revealing the point of diminishing returns.

Cost-effectiveness (CE) = system effectiveness ÷ (initial cost + sustainment cost); reliability and maintainability strongly drive sustainment cost. CE is distinct from cost–benefit analysis (CBA), which expresses outcomes in money. Life Cycle Cost (LCC) is the total cost across all stages (concept, development, production, utilization & support, retirement). LCC ≠ Total Cost of Ownership / Whole Life Cost, which often count only post-acquisition costs. Estimating methods: analogy, bottom-up, Delphi, design-to-cost (DTC), expert judgment, parametric/algorithmic (COSYSMO, COCOMO), Parkinsonian, price-to-win, taxonomy, top-down.

Agility engineering

Agility is the ability to thrive in uncertain, unpredictable environments. Effective response has four metrics: timely, affordable, predictable, comprehensive. Distinguish agile systems engineering (an agile process — §4.2.2) from engineering an agile system (the product — this section). Agility comes from a loosely-coupled, plug-and-play modular architecture with three elements: encapsulated modules, a passive infrastructure (minimal rules/standards enabling connectivity), and an active infrastructure (designated responsibilities: module-mix evolution, module readiness, situational awareness, system assembly, infrastructure evolution). Agility is measured by time, cost, predictability, and scope of response.

Human systems integration (HSI)

HSI integrates technology, organizations, and people effectively — the TOP model within an environment. It is a transdisciplinary, sociotechnical approach treating every system as containing both humans and machines. HSI rests on four communities of practice (human factors/ergonomics, IT, systems engineering, and the operational domain) and is delivered chiefly through Human-Centered Design (HCD) with human-in-the-loop (HITL) activities. Its purpose is to optimize total system performance and stakeholder satisfaction across technology, organization, people, and environment.

Interoperability analysis

Interoperability is the ability of a system to work with other systems to achieve an outcome (the handbook cites four standard definitions emphasising exchanging and using information). It is achieved two ways, which can be combined: adopting published standards ("plug and play") and/or defining a custom interface when no suitable standard exists. Interoperability is a key enabler of a System of Systems and increasingly important with IoT and coalition operations. (The Øresund Bridge illustrates two-nation interoperability challenges.)

Logistics engineering

Also called product-support engineering: identifying, acquiring, and provisioning all support resources to sustain operation and maintenance, and engineering the inherent supportability of the design. Scope: determine support requirements, design for supportability, acquire support, and provide cost-effective support during utilization. It evolved into supply-chain management (commercial) and integrated logistics support / ILS (defence). Supportability analysis uses functional/physical failure analysis (FMECA, FTA, RBD), task identification (corrective via FMECA, preventive via RCM), LORA (level of repair analysis), and recording & corrective action.

Manufacturability / producibility analysis

Enables production in a responsible, cost-effective manner. Producibility considerations differ with production volume (satellites vs. tanks vs. phones). A key task is deciding whether existing production enabling systems suffice; if not they must be developed in time — and the production enabling system can cost more than the system being produced. Multidisciplinary teams simplify the design and stabilize processes to cut risk, cost, lead time, and material use; long-lead, sole-source, and special-process items are evaluated early. (IKEA is the supply-chain/producibility exemplar.)

Reliability, availability, maintainability (RAM)

RAM (or RMA) are interrelated QCs best treated as non-functional requirements that often drive other requirements, so plan them early and integrate with the SEMP. In many industries, availability is viewed as the most important to a user/acquirer because availability loss translates directly to mission/production loss.

Reliability = "the ability of a system to perform as designed, without failure, in an operational environment, for a stated period of time." Reliability engineering's four objectives, in priority order: prevent/reduce failures; identify and correct causes; cope with failures that occur; estimate reliability of new designs. Modern design-for-reliability shifts from reactive "test-analyze-fix" to proactive prevention. Watch the metric trap: MTBF is not "expected life"; prefer success probability at a time, or failure-free period.

Availability = uptime ÷ total time, governed by reliability (uptime) and maintainability + logistics (downtime). Three forms:

  • Inherent (Ai) — design reliability + maintainability only; ideal support; excludes preventive maintenance and all delays.
  • Achieved (Aa) — adds preventive (scheduled) maintenance; still excludes logistics/administrative delays.
  • Operational (Ao) — real environment; includes logistics and administrative delay times.

Maintainability is the built-in ability to be restored (maintenance is the action; maintainability is designed in). Maintenance types: corrective (unscheduled, after failure), preventive (scheduled), predictive (condition-based), and upgrades. Key metric: MTTR (mean time to repair), typically lognormally distributed.

Exam focus — RAM
The three availability types are a classic question. Memorize what each one includes: Ai = design only, Aa = + preventive maintenance, Ao = + logistics & administrative delays. Also remember reliability ≠ MTBF-as-lifetime, and maintainability (designed) ≠ maintenance (action).

Resilience engineering

Resilience = providing required capability when facing adversity; it typically subsumes survivability. SE resilience identifies the required capabilities, the adverse conditions, and the architecture/design that ensures delivery. Crucially, resilience focuses on capability, not preserving the architecture — both continuity and adaptability are valid means.

Resilience has a two-layer taxonomy. Layer 1 — fundamental objectives: avoid, withstand, recover. Layer 2 — means objectives (adaptive response, agility, anticipation, constrain, continuity, disaggregation, evolution, graceful degradation, integrity, prepare, prevent, re-architect, redeploy, robustness, situational awareness, tolerance, transform, understand). System-state transitions fall into three categories: robustness (maintain capability), tolerance (move to lower capability gracefully), and recovery (return to higher capability). Resilience requirements often take the form of a resilience scenario in the ConOps/OpsCon and should be set early.

Sustainability engineering

Supports the circular economy — sharing, reusing, repairing, recycling to extend product life and minimize waste, aligned with the UN's 17 Sustainable Development Goals. Environmental impact analysis spans the full life cycle; disposal analysis and design for disassembly maximize residual value and recyclability. Tools: the ISO 14000 series, Environmental Product Declarations (EPD), and life-cycle assessment/management (LCA, LCIA, LCO, LCM).

System safety engineering

Reduces and mitigates hazards to an acceptable level of risk — engineered systems are never 100% safe. Safety is an emergent property of the system in its real operational environment: it is not the sum of element-level safety/reliability, and is shaped by use, maintenance, and management. "Acceptably safe" varies by industry/country; common bars are regulatory compliance, ALARP ("as low as reasonably practicable"), or ALARP-or-better-than-the-alternative for military/medical systems. Regulators assess both the likelihood a hazard leads to harm and the severity of harm.

Control hierarchy (most to least preferred): eliminate the hazard by design > reduce likelihood/severity by design mitigations > operational/maintenance controls, warnings, training. A near miss is an unmitigated hazardous situation that happened to cause no harm — vital feedback. Most accidents are multi-causal, and a hazardous situation can arise without any element failure (design error, or misaligned mental models of designers/operators/maintainers). A safety management system and a strong safety culture sustain safety through life.

System security engineering (SSE)

Ensures the system can function under anomalous and disruptive events from misuse and malice, by analysing threats and vulnerabilities and mitigating risks to assets across the life cycle. Threats may be external/internal and unintentional (misuse) or intentional (malicious). Physical security protects against physical actions; cybersecurity protects the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information assets. Trustworthiness spans privacy, reliability, resilience, safety, and security. NIST SP 800-160 Vol. 1 & 2 (built on ISO/IEC/IEEE 15288 and this handbook) integrate security through the SE process rather than bolting it on.

Loss-driven systems engineering (LDSE)

Capability-driven SE can neglect potential losses. LDSE unifies the loss-driven QCs — resilience, safety, security, sustainability/disposability, availability — so they collaborate on the adversities, weaknesses/hazards/vulnerabilities, assets/losses, and coping mechanisms considered, and integrate their risks into one risk-management activity.

Systems engineering analyses and methods (§3.2)

Modeling, analysis, and simulation (MA&S)

The essential artifact is an explicit model — an idealized representation of one or more aspects of the as-is or to-be system. The three activities are distinct: modeling = conceiving/creating/refining models; analysis = systematic, reproducible examination to gain insight; simulation = using a model to predict/study behaviour. In this handbook, simulation always uses a digital model; any examination with a physical model is a test. SE primarily uses digital models, classified as formal (machine-readable, defined semantics — logical, quantitative, geometric, surrogate) or informal. Models are further characterized by breadth, granularity, and fidelity.

MBSE makes the model the "authoritative source of truth," separating representation from presentation and overcoming the document-centric problem of duplicated, hard-to-maintain information. Benefits include more explicit SE, better problem specification, rigorous design, early V&V to reduce risk, multi-user collaboration, change-impact assessment, and mastering complexity. Multidisciplinary coordination uses a hub-and-spokes pattern with an integrated system-model repository at the hub. Black-box (opaque) models hide IP while exposing an interface; white-box (transparent) models reveal internals.

Prototyping

A prototype enhances learning and understanding of needs and is set aside once that purpose is met — it is not the "first draft" of production. Rapid prototyping is fast and assembled from existing physical/graphical/mathematical elements (3-D printing, simulation shells); used for form, fit, human-system interface, operations, producibility. Traditional prototyping reduces risk/uncertainty and has two variants: a partial prototype verifies critical elements; a full prototype is a complete representation.

Traceability

"The ability to trace the history, application, or location of an item" (ISO 9000) — for SE, an association between items (needs, requirements, architecture, design, verification, validation, models). Types:

  • Bidirectional — a link that automatically establishes a reverse link back.
  • Vertical — across architectural/organizational levels ("parent/child").
  • Horizontal — across elements at one level ("peer") and across life-cycle stages/processes.

Traceability is facilitated by the Configuration Management process (unique, never-reused identifiers) and is central to the digital thread, letting a requirement be traced through functional and physical representations to specific configurations — and back.

Interface management

Identifies, defines, designs, and manages interfaces across the life cycle, cutting across Agreement, Technical Management, and Technical processes. Treat it as a distinct activity — most major issues found during integration, verification, and validation involve interfaces. The focus during requirements/architecture is on what crosses the boundary (the interaction characteristics), not how it is realized (deferred to Design). Definitions are captured in an Interface Control Document (ICD) (or IDD/IAD/DD) under configuration control, often via an Interface Control Working Group.

The N² diagram is the signature analysis tool: an N×N matrix with elements on the diagonal and interactions in the off-diagonal cells; interactions flow clockwise (outputs horizontal, inputs vertical); a blank cell = no interaction. The Design Structure Matrix (DSM) looks similar but uses the opposite (counterclockwise) convention. A coupling matrix (a type of N² diagram) groups elements into aggregates to minimize the interfaces verified during integration.

Exam focus — interfaces
Remember the N² convention: outputs row-wise (horizontal), inputs column-wise (vertical), flow clockwise. The DSM is the counterclockwise cousin. And the interface artifact name to know is the ICD.

Architecture frameworks

An architecture description framework is "a set of conventions, principles and practices for the description of architectures established within a specific domain or community" (ISO/IEC/IEEE 42010:2022). Frameworks provide viewpoints that address stakeholder concerns and yield views. Examples: TOGAF (IT), DoDAF / NAF / UAF (defence), Zachman (enterprise vs. product frameworks), UAM. Frameworks support governance at enterprise and project levels, management, description (42010/42020), and evaluation (42030) — with methods such as ATAM and MFESA.

Patterns

Patterns are recurrences — repeated regularities. Informal patterns live in expertise/tribal knowledge or prose templates (à la Alexander's civil-architecture patterns and the Gang-of-Four software patterns). Formal patterns are model-based and machine-processable — e.g., the S*Metamodel and reusable S*Patterns (configurable models of system families). Formal patterns answer "what is the smallest model needed to represent a system across its life cycle?" and capture truths such as "all behaviour is interaction-based" and "requirement statements are transfer functions."

Design thinking

A complement to systems thinking that centres on human needs, problem framing, and ideation, using successive divergent and convergent phases of synthesis and analysis (related to Axiomatic Design). It helps challenge assumptions, redefine problems, anticipate emergence, and generate innovative solutions across the early life-cycle processes.

Biomimicry

"A practice that learns from and mimics the strategies found in nature to solve human design challenges." Nature-inspired SE abstracts a function, finds natural systems that achieve it, abstracts the strategy, and transfers it to the system of interest — improving capability, efficiency, resilience, and environmental impact (e.g., Velcro from burdock, gecko-inspired grippers, compound-eye sensors).
